# Enlightenment Intellectual Network - Person Enumeration

**Date**: 2026-01-18
**Purpose**: Enumerate individuals for the Enlightenment historical network.

---

## Foundational Figures (Pre-1700 Influences)

These figures established the intellectual foundations upon which the 18th-century Enlightenment was built.

### John Locke (1632-1704)
- **Role**: Philosopher, physician, political theorist
- **Location**: England, Netherlands (exile 1683-1689)
- **Significance**: "Father of Liberalism." His empiricism (*Essay Concerning Human Understanding*) and political philosophy (*Two Treatises of Government*) established foundational concepts of natural rights, social contract, and religious toleration that influenced virtually all subsequent Enlightenment thinkers.
- **Key Works**: *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689), *Two Treatises of Government* (1689), *A Letter Concerning Toleration* (1689)
- **Connections**: Newton (friendship, theological correspondence), Shaftesbury (1st Earl, patron and collaborator), influenced Voltaire, Montesquieu, Hume, Rousseau, the American founders
- **Notes**: Bridge figure between Scientific Revolution and Enlightenment

### Isaac Newton (1643-1727)
- **Role**: Natural philosopher, mathematician
- **Location**: England (Cambridge, London)
- **Significance**: His *Principia Mathematica* established the mechanistic worldview and mathematical methodology that Enlightenment thinkers sought to extend to human affairs. Symbol of reason's triumph.
- **Key Works**: *Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica* (1687), *Opticks* (1704)
- **Connections**: Locke (friendship, theological correspondence), influenced Voltaire, d'Alembert, Kant
- **Notes**: More scientist than philosophe, but foundational influence on Enlightenment methodology

### Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646-1716)
- **Role**: Philosopher, mathematician, polymath
- **Location**: German states (Hanover), traveled widely
- **Significance**: Co-inventor of calculus, rationalist philosopher. His optimism, monadology, and principle of sufficient reason shaped German philosophy through Wolff. Target of Voltaire's satire in *Candide*.
- **Key Works**: *Theodicy* (1710), *Monadology* (1714), mathematical papers
- **Connections**: Founded Berlin Academy (1700), influenced Wolff, Kant; debated with Newton (calculus priority)
- **Notes**: His optimistic theodicy was widely mocked by later philosophes

## French Philosophes

The core group of French intellectuals centered around the *Encyclopédie* project and Parisian salons.

### Voltaire (François-Marie Arouet) (1694-1778) ⭐ SEED
- **Role**: Writer, philosopher, public intellectual
- **Location**: France, England (1726-1729), Prussia (1750-1753), Geneva/Ferney (1755-1778)
- **Significance**: The quintessential philosophe. Prolific writer with ~21,000 letters to ~1,800 correspondents. Championed civil liberties, freedom of religion, free trade. Popularized Newton and Locke in France.
- **Key Works**: *Letters on the English* (1733), *Candide* (1759), *Philosophical Dictionary* (1764), contributed ~45 articles to *Encyclopédie*
- **Connections**: d'Alembert (529 letters), Diderot (modest correspondence, met 1778), Frederick the Great (patron), Catherine the Great, Émilie du Châtelet (intellectual partner), met Franklin and Voltaire embraced (1778)
- **Notes**: 70% of correspondents were French despite cosmopolitan image

### Denis Diderot (1713-1784)
- **Role**: Philosopher, art critic, writer, editor
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: Chief editor of the *Encyclopédie* (1751-1772), the defining project of the French Enlightenment. Wrote thousands of articles. Later radical materialist.
- **Key Works**: *Encyclopédie* (editor), *Le Neveu de Rameau*, *Jacques le Fataliste*, *Le Fils naturel* (1757)
- **Connections**: d'Alembert (co-editor until 1758), Rousseau (early friendship, broke ~1757), d'Holbach (salon regular), Voltaire (modest correspondence), Sophie Volland (correspondent/lover)
- **Notes**: Break with Rousseau over theater and moral philosophy

### Jean le Rond d'Alembert (1717-1783)
- **Role**: Mathematician, philosopher, encyclopedist
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: Co-editor of *Encyclopédie* until 1758 (wrote the famous "Preliminary Discourse"). Major mathematician (wave equation, d'Alembert's principle). Permanent secretary of Académie française.
- **Key Works**: *Preliminary Discourse to the Encyclopedia* (1751), *Traité de dynamique* (1743), mathematical papers
- **Connections**: Diderot (co-editor), Voltaire (529 letters), Frederick the Great, Lagrange (correspondence), Julie de Lespinasse (salon hostess, close relationship)
- **Notes**: Withdrew from *Encyclopédie* in 1758 after controversies

### Montesquieu (Charles-Louis de Secondat) (1689-1755) ⭐ SEED
- **Role**: Political philosopher, jurist
- **Location**: Bordeaux, Paris, traveled to England (1729-1731)
- **Significance**: *The Spirit of the Laws* (1748) articulated separation of powers and comparative political analysis. Profound influence on American constitutional framers.
- **Key Works**: *Persian Letters* (1721), *The Spirit of the Laws* (1748)
- **Connections**: Académie de Bordeaux (member 1716), Académie française (elected 1728), influenced Madison, Hamilton, Jefferson
- **Notes**: *Spirit of Laws* placed on Catholic Index in 1751

### Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712-1778) ⭐ SEED
- **Role**: Philosopher, composer, writer
- **Location**: Geneva (native), Paris, various exiles
- **Significance**: Controversial figure whose *Social Contract* and *Emile* influenced political theory and education. Critique of civilization and ideas about "general will" made him both central and contested.
- **Key Works**: *Discourse on Inequality* (1755), *The Social Contract* (1762), *Emile* (1762), *Confessions* (posthumous)
- **Connections**: Diderot (early friendship, broke ~1757), d'Holbach (attended salon, later estranged), Hume (quarrel 1766), Voltaire (antagonistic), contributed music articles to *Encyclopédie*
- **Notes**: Famous quarrels with Diderot, d'Holbach circle, and Hume. Deist vs. materialist tensions.

### Baron d'Holbach (Paul-Henri Thiry) (1723-1789)
- **Role**: Philosopher, salon host
- **Location**: Paris (rue Royale)
- **Significance**: Host of the most radical salon of the Enlightenment ("la coterie holbachique"). Systematic atheist and materialist. Published influential atheist works anonymously.
- **Key Works**: *The System of Nature* (1770), *Christianity Unveiled* (1761), contributed chemistry/mineralogy articles to *Encyclopédie*
- **Connections**: Diderot (close friend, salon regular), d'Alembert, Helvétius, Condorcet, Rousseau (attended, later estranged), Hume (visitor)
- **Notes**: Salon met twice weekly (Sundays and Thursdays) from ~1750-1790

### Claude-Adrien Helvétius (1715-1771)
- **Role**: Philosopher, tax farmer
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: His *De l'esprit* (1758) caused scandal with its utilitarian, anti-religious ethics. Condemned by Sorbonne and ordered burned. Influenced Bentham.
- **Key Works**: *De l'esprit* (1758), *De l'homme* (posthumous)
- **Connections**: d'Holbach (salon friend), Diderot, his widow (Anne-Catherine) hosted influential salon that Franklin attended
- **Notes**: His salon continued under his widow after his death

### Étienne Bonnot de Condillac (1714-1780)
- **Role**: Philosopher, epistemologist
- **Location**: France
- **Significance**: Developed Locke's empiricism into sensationalism—all knowledge derives from transformed sensations. Major influence on French psychology and linguistics.
- **Key Works**: *Essay on the Origin of Human Knowledge* (1746), *Treatise on Sensations* (1754)
- **Connections**: Diderot, d'Alembert, Rousseau (tutored his nephew), contributed to *Encyclopédie*
- **Notes**: Brother of the Abbé de Mably

### Nicolas de Condorcet (1743-1794)
- **Role**: Mathematician, philosopher, political theorist
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: Bridged mathematics and social science. Applied probability to voting theory ("Condorcet paradox"). Permanent Secretary of Académie des Sciences. Champion of women's rights, public education, and progress.
- **Key Works**: *Essay on the Application of Analysis to the Probability of Majority Decisions* (1785), *Sketch for a Historical Picture of the Progress of the Human Mind* (1795)
- **Connections**: Turgot (mentor, close friend), d'Alembert, d'Holbach salon, Jefferson (met in Paris), wrote biography of Turgot
- **Notes**: Died during French Revolution; his *Sketch* is a defining statement of Enlightenment progress

### Julien Offray de La Mettrie (1709-1751)
- **Role**: Physician, philosopher
- **Location**: France, Netherlands (exile), Prussia (Frederick's court)
- **Significance**: Radical materialist who argued humans are machines (*L'Homme Machine*). Pushed Enlightenment materialism to its logical extreme.
- **Key Works**: *Natural History of the Soul* (1745), *Man a Machine* (1747)
- **Connections**: Frederick the Great (patron after exile), influenced d'Holbach
- **Notes**: Died at Frederick's court; too radical even for many philosophes

### Louis de Jaucourt (1704-1779)
- **Role**: Encyclopedist, physician
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: Most prolific contributor to *Encyclopédie*—wrote ~17,288 articles (about 25% of total text) on topics from medicine to politics.
- **Key Works**: *Encyclopédie* contributions (17,288 articles)
- **Connections**: Diderot, d'Alembert
- **Notes**: Unpaid work; sacrificed personal fortune for the project

### Étienne Noël Damilaville (1723-1768)
- **Role**: Tax official, intermediary
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: Key intermediary in philosophe network. Helped circulate letters and clandestine texts when censorship made direct communication difficult.
- **Key Works**: Minor; significance is as network connector
- **Connections**: Voltaire, Diderot, d'Alembert, d'Holbach—served as intermediary for all
- **Notes**: Used his position in tax administration to facilitate correspondence

### André Morellet (1727-1819)
- **Role**: Economist, writer, translator
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: Translated Adam Smith into French. Contributed to *Encyclopédie* on theology and philosophy. Member of d'Holbach's circle.
- **Key Works**: *Encyclopédie* articles, translation of *Wealth of Nations*
- **Connections**: d'Holbach salon, Turgot, translated Adam Smith
- **Notes**: Lived through Revolution; wrote memoirs of salon culture

## Scottish Enlightenment

The remarkable concentration of intellectual talent in 18th-century Edinburgh and Glasgow.

### David Hume (1711-1776) ⭐ SEED
- **Role**: Philosopher, historian, essayist
- **Location**: Edinburgh, travels to France (secretary in Paris embassy 1763-1766)
- **Significance**: Greatest empiricist philosopher. His skepticism about causation and religion profoundly influenced Kant ("awakened from dogmatic slumber"). Friendship with Adam Smith central to Scottish Enlightenment.
- **Key Works**: *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739-40), *Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* (1748), *Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion* (posthumous), *History of England*
- **Connections**: Adam Smith (close friend), d'Alembert, d'Holbach (visited salon), Rousseau (quarrel 1766), influenced Kant
- **Notes**: Famous quarrel with Rousseau in 1766; the dispute went public across Europe

### Adam Smith (1723-1790) ⭐ SEED
- **Role**: Moral philosopher, economist
- **Location**: Glasgow, Edinburgh, Kirkcaldy; France (1764-1766)
- **Significance**: Founded classical economics with *Wealth of Nations*. His moral philosophy (*Theory of Moral Sentiments*) deeply influenced by Hume. Central figure linking philosophy and economics.
- **Key Works**: *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759), *An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations* (1776)
- **Connections**: Hume (closest friend), Hutcheson (teacher), met Voltaire, Quesnay, Turgot in France, influenced Bentham
- **Notes**: Met Physiocrats in Paris; *Wealth of Nations* critiques and absorbs their ideas

### Francis Hutcheson (1694-1746)
- **Role**: Philosopher, professor
- **Location**: Dublin (born Ulster), Glasgow (professor from 1729)
- **Significance**: "Father of the Scottish Enlightenment." His moral sense theory influenced Hume and Smith. Teacher of Adam Smith at Glasgow.
- **Key Works**: *Inquiry into the Original of Our Ideas of Beauty and Virtue* (1725), *A System of Moral Philosophy* (1755)
- **Connections**: Adam Smith (teacher), influenced Hume, anticipated utilitarianism
- **Notes**: Bridge between Shaftesbury's moral sense theory and Scottish school

### Thomas Reid (1710-1796)
- **Role**: Philosopher
- **Location**: Aberdeen, Glasgow (succeeded Smith as professor 1764)
- **Significance**: Founder of Scottish Common Sense philosophy. Criticized Hume's skepticism, arguing humans have reliable innate beliefs. Influenced American philosophy.
- **Key Works**: *Inquiry into the Human Mind on the Principles of Common Sense* (1764), *Essays on the Intellectual Powers of Man* (1785)
- **Connections**: Succeeded Adam Smith at Glasgow, opposed Hume philosophically
- **Notes**: Major influence on American college philosophy curricula

### Adam Ferguson (1723-1816)
- **Role**: Philosopher, historian
- **Location**: Edinburgh
- **Significance**: Pioneer of sociology ("father of sociology"). Analyzed civil society and warned about commercial society's effects on civic virtue.
- **Key Works**: *Essay on the History of Civil Society* (1767), *History of the Progress and Termination of the Roman Republic* (1783)
- **Connections**: Hume, Smith, member of Edinburgh clubs (Select Society, Poker Club)
- **Notes**: Professor of moral philosophy at Edinburgh

### William Robertson (1721-1793)
- **Role**: Historian, clergyman, administrator
- **Location**: Edinburgh
- **Significance**: Leading historian of the British Enlightenment. Principal of University of Edinburgh (1762-1793). Reformed the university's curriculum and reputation.
- **Key Works**: *History of Scotland* (1759), *History of the Reign of Emperor Charles V* (1769), *History of America* (1777)
- **Connections**: Hume, Smith, Ferguson, leader of Church of Scotland's "Moderate Party"
- **Notes**: One of Britain's most widely read historians in Europe

### Henry Home, Lord Kames (1696-1782)
- **Role**: Judge, philosopher, agricultural improver
- **Location**: Edinburgh
- **Significance**: Patron and mentor to many Scottish Enlightenment figures. Wrote on law, aesthetics, morality. Employed young Adam Smith and encouraged Hume.
- **Key Works**: *Essays on the Principles of Morality and Natural Religion* (1751), *Elements of Criticism* (1762)
- **Connections**: Hume (mentor), Smith (patron), Reid, Ferguson
- **Notes**: Key institutional figure; helped establish Edinburgh's intellectual culture

### Dugald Stewart (1753-1828)
- **Role**: Philosopher
- **Location**: Edinburgh
- **Significance**: Transmitted Scottish Enlightenment philosophy to the 19th century. Biographer of Smith and Reid. Taught many influential students.
- **Key Works**: *Elements of the Philosophy of the Human Mind* (1792-1827), biographies of Smith, Reid, Robertson
- **Connections**: Student of Reid and Ferguson, wrote Smith's biography
- **Notes**: Late figure; bridge to 19th century

## German Aufklärung

The German Enlightenment, centered in Prussia and university towns.

### Immanuel Kant (1724-1804) ⭐ SEED
- **Role**: Philosopher
- **Location**: Königsberg (never left East Prussia)
- **Significance**: Synthesized rationalism and empiricism in critical philosophy. His essay "What is Enlightenment?" (1784) defined the movement: "Sapere aude" (Dare to know). Transformed philosophy.
- **Key Works**: *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781), *Critique of Practical Reason* (1788), *Critique of Judgment* (1790), "What is Enlightenment?" (1784)
- **Connections**: Mendelssohn (correspondent, competitor), awakened by Hume, influenced by Rousseau on ethics, Wolff (early training)
- **Notes**: Mendelssohn visited Kant in 1777; they competed for Berlin Academy prize (1763)

### Christian Wolff (1679-1754)
- **Role**: Philosopher, mathematician
- **Location**: Halle, Marburg
- **Significance**: Dominant German philosopher pre-Kant. Systematized Leibniz's ideas. His rationalism dominated German universities until Kant's critical turn.
- **Key Works**: *Rational Thoughts on God, the World, and the Soul of Man* (German Logic, 1713), systematic philosophy treatises
- **Connections**: Leibniz (influence), taught generation of German academics, Kant (early training in Wolffian philosophy)
- **Notes**: Expelled from Halle (1723) by Pietists; recalled by Frederick the Great

### Moses Mendelssohn (1729-1786)
- **Role**: Philosopher, Jewish community leader
- **Location**: Berlin
- **Significance**: Leading figure of German Enlightenment and Jewish Haskalah (Jewish Enlightenment). Championed religious tolerance and Jewish emancipation. "German Socrates."
- **Key Works**: *Phädon* (1767), *Jerusalem* (1783), essays on aesthetics and metaphysics
- **Connections**: Kant (correspondent, won over Kant in 1763 prize), Lessing (close friend), Frederick the Great
- **Notes**: Answered "What is Enlightenment?" same year as Kant (1784)

### Gotthold Ephraim Lessing (1729-1781)
- **Role**: Writer, dramatist, critic
- **Location**: Various German cities, Wolfenbüttel
- **Significance**: Germany's greatest Enlightenment dramatist. Championed religious tolerance (*Nathan the Wise*). Major literary and aesthetic critic.
- **Key Works**: *Nathan the Wise* (1779), *Laocoon* (1766), *Hamburg Dramaturgy*, theological writings
- **Connections**: Mendelssohn (close friend), Herder, Frederick the Great
- **Notes**: *Nathan the Wise* features a parable of religious tolerance (Ring Parable)

### Johann Gottfried Herder (1744-1803)
- **Role**: Philosopher, theologian, literary critic
- **Location**: Riga, Weimar
- **Significance**: Transitional figure toward Romanticism. Emphasized culture, language, history, and Volksgeist (national spirit). Critiqued abstract universalism.
- **Key Works**: *Treatise on the Origin of Language* (1772), *Ideas for the Philosophy of History of Humanity* (1784-91)
- **Connections**: Kant (student), Goethe (friend at Weimar), influenced Romantic movement
- **Notes**: Student of Kant but moved away from Enlightenment universalism

### Alexander Gottlieb Baumgarten (1714-1762)
- **Role**: Philosopher
- **Location**: Frankfurt an der Oder
- **Significance**: Founded aesthetics as a philosophical discipline. His *Aesthetica* coined the term. Influenced Kant's aesthetic theory.
- **Key Works**: *Aesthetica* (1750-58), *Metaphysica* (textbook Kant used)
- **Connections**: Wolff (follower), Kant used his *Metaphysica* as teaching text
- **Notes**: Early death limited his output

### Johann Wolfgang von Goethe (1749-1832)
- **Role**: Writer, poet, natural philosopher, statesman
- **Location**: Frankfurt (birth), Leipzig, Strasbourg, Weimar (from 1775)
- **Significance**: Germany's greatest literary figure. Early works (*Sorrows of Young Werther*, *Götz von Berlichingen*) sparked Sturm und Drang movement. Transitional figure between Enlightenment and Romanticism.
- **Key Works**: *The Sorrows of Young Werther* (1774), *Faust* (Part I 1808), scientific writings
- **Connections**: Herder (met 1770, lifelong friendship), wider German intellectual world
- **Notes**: Primarily included for Herder connection; literary stature transcends "Enlightenment" category

## British Empiricists and Political Thinkers

English philosophers and political theorists, including the dissenting tradition.

### George Berkeley (1685-1753)
- **Role**: Philosopher, Anglican bishop
- **Location**: Ireland, England, Rhode Island (briefly)
- **Significance**: Developed idealist empiricism: "to be is to be perceived." Critiqued Locke's theory of abstract ideas. Influenced Hume.
- **Key Works**: *A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge* (1710), *Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous* (1713)
- **Connections**: Locke (built on and critiqued), influenced Hume
- **Notes**: His idealism was often seen as paradoxical or absurd

### Anthony Ashley Cooper, 3rd Earl of Shaftesbury (1671-1713)
- **Role**: Philosopher, politician
- **Location**: England
- **Significance**: Developed moral sense theory—humans have innate moral sentiments. Influenced Hutcheson and through him the Scottish school. Critiqued Locke's rejection of innate ideas.
- **Key Works**: *Characteristics of Men, Manners, Opinions, Times* (1711)
- **Connections**: Locke (educated by, later critiqued), influenced Hutcheson, Diderot
- **Notes**: Grandson of Locke's patron (1st Earl)

### Bernard Mandeville (1670-1733)
- **Role**: Philosopher, satirist
- **Location**: Netherlands (born), England
- **Significance**: *The Fable of the Bees* argued private vices produce public benefits—scandal that influenced economic thought. Provoked Hutcheson's moral philosophy.
- **Key Works**: *The Fable of the Bees* (1714, expanded 1723)
- **Connections**: Provoked responses from Hutcheson, Berkeley; influenced Smith's economics
- **Notes**: Controversial figure; accused of promoting vice

### Edmund Burke (1729-1797)
- **Role**: Statesman, political philosopher
- **Location**: Ireland (born), England
- **Significance**: Leading conservative critic of the French Revolution (*Reflections*). Earlier work on aesthetics (*Sublime and Beautiful*). Represents Enlightenment's limits and counter-currents.
- **Key Works**: *A Philosophical Enquiry into the Sublime and Beautiful* (1757), *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790)
- **Connections**: Opposed Paine, influenced by Montesquieu; tensions with radical Enlightenment
- **Notes**: Shows Enlightenment's internal tensions—reason vs. tradition

### Joseph Priestley (1733-1804)
- **Role**: Scientist, theologian, political theorist
- **Location**: England, Pennsylvania (emigrated 1794)
- **Significance**: Discovered oxygen (independently). Materialist, determinist, religious dissenter. Championed toleration, education reform, individual liberty.
- **Key Works**: Scientific papers on gases, *History of Electricity*, theological works
- **Connections**: Franklin (correspondence, scientific exchanges), Bentham (influenced), Lavoisier (parallel oxygen discovery)
- **Notes**: Birmingham Riots (1791) destroyed his home; fled to America

### Jeremy Bentham (1748-1832)
- **Role**: Philosopher, legal reformer
- **Location**: London
- **Significance**: Founder of utilitarianism—"greatest happiness of the greatest number." Developed systematic approach to legal and social reform.
- **Key Works**: *Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation* (1789), numerous reform proposals
- **Connections**: Influenced by Helvétius, Priestley; Smith; Beccaria (criminal law reform)
- **Notes**: Late Enlightenment figure; influence extends into 19th century

### Thomas Paine (1737-1809)
- **Role**: Political theorist, pamphleteer
- **Location**: England, American colonies, France
- **Significance**: Most widely read Enlightenment radical. *Common Sense* catalyzed American independence. *Rights of Man* defended French Revolution. *Age of Reason* promoted deism.
- **Key Works**: *Common Sense* (1776), *Rights of Man* (1791), *The Age of Reason* (1794)
- **Connections**: Franklin (mentor, helped him emigrate), Jefferson, opposed Burke
- **Notes**: Bridged American, British, and French revolutionary discourse

### Cesare Beccaria (1738-1794)
- **Role**: Jurist, philosopher
- **Location**: Milan
- **Significance**: *On Crimes and Punishments* (1764) revolutionized criminal justice—opposed torture and death penalty, promoted proportional punishment. Major influence on legal reform.
- **Key Works**: *On Crimes and Punishments* (1764)
- **Connections**: Influenced Voltaire, Bentham, Jefferson; translated across Europe
- **Notes**: Italian Enlightenment figure with Europe-wide impact

## Physiocrats and Economists

Economic theorists, particularly the French Physiocrats.

### François Quesnay (1694-1774)
- **Role**: Physician, economist
- **Location**: Paris, Versailles (physician to Madame de Pompadour, Louis XV)
- **Significance**: Founder of Physiocracy—first systematic school of economics. Developed *Tableau économique* modeling economic flows. Contributed economic articles to *Encyclopédie*.
- **Key Works**: *Tableau économique* (1758), *Encyclopédie* articles ("Farmers," "Grains")
- **Connections**: Turgot, Mirabeau (the elder), Smith (met in Paris), Diderot (Encyclopédie)
- **Notes**: Court physician gave him access to power

### Anne-Robert-Jacques Turgot (1727-1781)
- **Role**: Economist, administrator
- **Location**: Limoges (intendant), Paris (Controller-General 1774-76)
- **Significance**: Attempted Enlightenment economic reforms as Controller-General—free trade in grain, abolition of guilds. Major economic theorist beyond strict Physiocracy.
- **Key Works**: *Reflections on the Formation and Distribution of Wealth* (1766), *Encyclopédie* articles
- **Connections**: Condorcet (close friend, protégé), Quesnay, Adam Smith, d'Alembert
- **Notes**: Reform efforts blocked by vested interests; dismissed 1776

### Victor de Riqueti, Marquis de Mirabeau (1715-1789)
- **Role**: Economist, political writer
- **Location**: France
- **Significance**: Leading Physiocrat; coined term "civilization." Father of the revolutionary leader Mirabeau.
- **Key Works**: *L'Ami des hommes* (1756), *Philosophie rurale* (with Quesnay, 1763)
- **Connections**: Quesnay (collaborator), Turgot
- **Notes**: "Friend of Mankind"—one of most widely read economic writers

## American Enlightenment

Colonial and early American thinkers influenced by and contributing to Enlightenment thought.

### Benjamin Franklin (1706-1790)
- **Role**: Scientist, diplomat, inventor, writer
- **Location**: Philadelphia, London, Paris (1776-1785)
- **Significance**: Exemplar of Enlightenment values—scientist, inventor, self-made man. His electrical experiments brought international fame. In Paris, bridged American and French Enlightenment.
- **Key Works**: *Experiments and Observations on Electricity* (1751), *Autobiography*, *Poor Richard's Almanack*
- **Connections**: Voltaire (met 1778, famous embrace), Madame Helvétius (salon friend), Condorcet, introduced Jefferson to Paris salons, Priestley (correspondence)
- **Notes**: Arrived Paris 1776; "Behold the man who tamed the lightning!"

### Thomas Jefferson (1743-1826)
- **Role**: Statesman, philosopher, architect
- **Location**: Virginia, Paris (1784-1789)
- **Significance**: Author of Declaration of Independence. Minister to France, immersed in philosophe circles. Embodied Enlightenment ideals of reason, natural rights, religious freedom.
- **Key Works**: *Declaration of Independence* (1776), *Notes on the State of Virginia* (1785), *Virginia Statute for Religious Freedom* (1786)
- **Connections**: Franklin (introduced him to Paris salons), Condorcet, influenced by Locke, Montesquieu
- **Notes**: Paris years (1784-89) transformed his intellectual horizons

### James Madison (1751-1836)
- **Role**: Statesman, political theorist
- **Location**: Virginia
- **Significance**: "Father of the Constitution." Applied Enlightenment political theory (especially Montesquieu) to constitutional design. *Federalist Papers* are classic of political philosophy.
- **Key Works**: *Federalist Papers* (with Hamilton, Jay), Constitutional Convention notes
- **Connections**: Jefferson (close collaborator), influenced by Montesquieu, Hume
- **Notes**: Extended Montesquieu's analysis to large republics

### John Adams (1735-1826)
- **Role**: Statesman, political theorist
- **Location**: Massachusetts, Europe (diplomatic missions)
- **Significance**: Constitutional theorist emphasizing balanced government. More skeptical of human nature than Jefferson.
- **Key Works**: *Thoughts on Government* (1776), *Defence of the Constitutions* (1787-88)
- **Connections**: Franklin, Jefferson; influenced by classical republics and Enlightenment theory
- **Notes**: Ambassador to Britain; met philosophes in Paris

## Scientific-Mathematical Circle

Natural philosophers and mathematicians whose work intersected with Enlightenment thought.

### Leonhard Euler (1707-1783)
- **Role**: Mathematician
- **Location**: Basel, St. Petersburg, Berlin
- **Significance**: Most prolific mathematician in history. Worked at Berlin Academy under Frederick the Great. Advanced calculus, number theory, mechanics, optics.
- **Key Works**: Hundreds of papers and books on mathematics, physics, astronomy
- **Connections**: d'Alembert (correspondent, sometimes rival), Frederick the Great (patron), Berlin Academy
- **Notes**: Returned to Russia 1766 after conflicts with Frederick

### Pierre-Simon Laplace (1749-1827)
- **Role**: Mathematician, astronomer
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: Developed celestial mechanics and probability theory. When asked about God's role in his system, reportedly said: "I had no need of that hypothesis."
- **Key Works**: *Mécanique céleste* (1799-1825), *Théorie analytique des probabilités* (1812)
- **Connections**: d'Alembert (mentor), Condorcet, Lavoisier
- **Notes**: Late Enlightenment; bridges to 19th century

### Antoine Lavoisier (1743-1794)
- **Role**: Chemist
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: "Father of modern chemistry." Identified oxygen's role in combustion, established chemical nomenclature. Member of Académie des Sciences.
- **Key Works**: *Elements of Chemistry* (1789)
- **Connections**: Priestley (parallel oxygen discovery, scientific exchange), Condorcet, Laplace
- **Notes**: Guillotined during Terror despite scientific eminence

### Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon (1707-1788)
- **Role**: Naturalist
- **Location**: Paris (Jardin du Roi)
- **Significance**: *Histoire naturelle* (44 volumes) was most widely read scientific work of the century. Speculated on earth's age and species transformation.
- **Key Works**: *Histoire naturelle* (1749-1804)
- **Connections**: Diderot, d'Alembert (Encyclopédie connections), influenced later evolutionary thought
- **Notes**: Challenged biblical timescales; diplomatic about religious implications

### Émilie du Châtelet (1706-1749)
- **Role**: Natural philosopher, mathematician, translator
- **Location**: France
- **Significance**: Translated Newton's *Principia* into French (still the standard French translation). Collaborated with Voltaire on Newtonian physics. Important scientist in her own right.
- **Key Works**: Translation of *Principia* (1759), *Institutions de physique* (1740)
- **Connections**: Voltaire (intellectual partner and lover), Maupertuis, Clairaut
- **Notes**: Rare female intellectual accepted by philosophes; died in childbirth

### Joseph-Louis Lagrange (1736-1813)
- **Role**: Mathematician, astronomer
- **Location**: Turin, Berlin (1766-1787), Paris (1787-1813)
- **Significance**: One of the greatest mathematicians of the 18th century. Developed calculus of variations, Lagrangian mechanics. Succeeded Euler at Berlin Academy.
- **Key Works**: *Mécanique analytique* (1788), papers on number theory and celestial mechanics
- **Connections**: Euler (predecessor at Berlin), d'Alembert (correspondent, helped his career), Frederick the Great (patron)
- **Notes**: Bridge between mid-century mathematical physics and 19th-century developments

## Salon Hostesses and Cultural Figures

Women who shaped Enlightenment discourse through salons.

### Madame Geoffrin (Marie-Thérèse Rodet Geoffrin) (1699-1777)
- **Role**: Salon hostess
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: Hosted one of the most influential Enlightenment salons. Financially supported the *Encyclopédie*. Connected philosophes with aristocrats and foreigners.
- **Key Works**: None (influence through salon)
- **Connections**: Diderot, d'Alembert, Montesquieu, foreign correspondents
- **Notes**: Monday (artists) and Wednesday (philosophes) salons

### Julie de Lespinasse (1732-1776)
- **Role**: Salon hostess
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: Hosted salon frequented by encyclopedists and philosophes. Close relationship with d'Alembert.
- **Key Works**: Letters
- **Connections**: d'Alembert (close relationship), Condorcet, Turgot met at her salon
- **Notes**: Former companion to Madame du Deffand; established rival salon

### Madame du Deffand (Marie de Vichy-Chamrond) (1697-1780)
- **Role**: Salon hostess, letter-writer
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: Hosted literary salon for decades. Famous correspondence with Voltaire, Horace Walpole.
- **Key Works**: Letters
- **Connections**: Voltaire, Montesquieu, d'Alembert (before break)
- **Notes**: Went blind 1752; dictated famous letters

### Madame Helvétius (Anne-Catherine de Ligniville) (1722-1800)
- **Role**: Salon hostess
- **Location**: Auteuil (outside Paris)
- **Significance**: After husband's death (1771), continued influential salon. Franklin was close friend (proposed marriage). Connected American visitors to French intellectuals.
- **Key Works**: None
- **Connections**: Franklin (close friend), Jefferson (visitor), Condorcet, Cabanis
- **Notes**: Her salon continued into Revolutionary period

## Enlightened Despots and Patrons

Rulers who patronized and corresponded with philosophes.

### Frederick II (the Great) of Prussia (1712-1786)
- **Role**: King of Prussia, patron of arts and philosophy
- **Location**: Prussia (Potsdam, Berlin)
- **Significance**: Model "enlightened despot." Corresponded with Voltaire, hosted him (1750-53). Revitalized Berlin Academy. Patron of Euler, La Mettrie. Wrote "Anti-Machiavel."
- **Key Works**: *Anti-Machiavel* (1740), flute compositions, poetry
- **Connections**: Voltaire (extensive correspondence, guest 1750-53), d'Alembert, La Mettrie (patron), Euler
- **Notes**: Relationship with Voltaire ended badly but correspondence resumed

### Catherine II (the Great) of Russia (1729-1796)
- **Role**: Empress of Russia
- **Location**: Russia (St. Petersburg)
- **Significance**: Corresponded with Voltaire and Diderot. Purchased Diderot's library (letting him keep it). Attempted Enlightenment-inspired reforms.
- **Key Works**: Correspondence, legal reform projects
- **Connections**: Voltaire (correspondent), Diderot (visited 1773-74), d'Alembert (invited)
- **Notes**: Called Voltaire "my master"; purchased his library after his death
