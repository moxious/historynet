# Enlightenment Intellectual Network - Organizations and Entities

**Date**: 2026-01-18
**Purpose**: Identify organizations, movements, and institutions for the Enlightenment network.

---

## Overview

This document catalogs the collective entities—formal organizations, informal movements, schools of thought, and intellectual projects—that structured Enlightenment thought. Entities serve as:
- **Institutional anchors** connecting individuals through membership
- **Intellectual currents** linking thinkers through shared ideas
- **Collaborative projects** requiring coordination among contributors

---

## 1. The Encyclopédie Project

### Encyclopédie (Project/Institution)
- **ID**: `entity-encyclopedie-project`
- **Type**: Collaborative project / publishing venture
- **Dates**: 1745-1772
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: The defining collective enterprise of the French Enlightenment. Brought together philosophers, scientists, artists, and craftsmen to create a comprehensive reference work embodying Enlightenment values.
- **Leadership**: Denis Diderot (editor 1745-1772), Jean le Rond d'Alembert (co-editor 1745-1758)
- **Key Members/Contributors**:
  - Jaucourt (~17,288 articles)
  - Voltaire (~45 articles)
  - Rousseau (music articles)
  - d'Holbach (chemistry, mineralogy)
  - Quesnay (economics)
  - Turgot (economics)
  - Condillac (philosophy)
- **Patrons**: Madame Geoffrin (financial support), Madame de Pompadour (protection at court)
- **Conflicts**: Suppressed twice (1752, 1759); d'Alembert withdrew 1758; Rousseau broke with project
- **Output**: 17 volumes of text, 11 volumes of plates, ~72,000 articles
- **Legacy**: Model for modern encyclopedias; spread Enlightenment ideas across Europe
- **Notes**: Central node of the French Enlightenment network—connecting nearly every major figure.

## 2. Schools of Thought and Intellectual Movements

### French Philosophes
- **ID**: `entity-philosophes`
- **Type**: Intellectual movement / informal group
- **Dates**: ~1730-1789
- **Location**: Primarily Paris
- **Significance**: The core group of French Enlightenment intellectuals. Not a formal organization but a network of writers, thinkers, and publicists who shared commitments to reason, reform, and critique of traditional authority.
- **Defining Characteristics**:
  - Critical of religious intolerance
  - Committed to empiricism and natural philosophy
  - Engaged in public intellectual life
  - Used print to reach broad audiences
- **Core Members**: Voltaire, Diderot, d'Alembert, Rousseau (ambivalently), d'Holbach, Helvétius, Condillac, Condorcet
- **Meeting Places**: Salons (Geoffrin, d'Holbach, Lespinasse), Café Procope
- **Notes**: Term used by contemporaries; united by ethos rather than doctrine.

### Scottish Enlightenment
- **ID**: `entity-scottish-enlightenment`
- **Type**: Intellectual movement
- **Dates**: ~1720-1800
- **Location**: Edinburgh, Glasgow
- **Significance**: Remarkable flowering of philosophy, history, economics, and social thought in 18th-century Scotland. Characterized by sociability, institutional innovation, and distinctive philosophical approaches.
- **Defining Characteristics**:
  - Emphasis on moral sense and sentiment
  - "Science of man" approach to human nature
  - Philosophical history and stadial theory
  - Practical focus on improvement
- **Core Members**: Hutcheson, Hume, Adam Smith, Ferguson, Reid, Robertson, Kames, Stewart
- **Key Institutions**: Universities of Edinburgh and Glasgow, Select Society, Poker Club
- **Notes**: Often seen as distinct from French Enlightenment in tone—less anti-clerical, more focused on commercial society.

### German Aufklärung
- **ID**: `entity-aufklarung`
- **Type**: Intellectual movement
- **Dates**: ~1700-1800
- **Location**: German-speaking territories (Prussia, Saxony, etc.)
- **Significance**: German Enlightenment, with distinctive characteristics including greater integration with Protestant theology and university philosophy.
- **Defining Characteristics**:
  - Rationalist philosophy (Wolffian school) until Kant
  - Religious reform and tolerance
  - Bureaucratic reform through enlightened government
  - Culmination in Kant's critical philosophy
- **Core Members**: Wolff, Kant, Mendelssohn, Lessing, Herder, Baumgarten
- **Key Institutions**: Berlin Academy, German universities (Halle, Königsberg)
- **Notes**: Kant's 1784 essay "What is Enlightenment?" defined the movement for posterity.

### Haskalah (Jewish Enlightenment)
- **ID**: `entity-haskalah`
- **Type**: Intellectual movement
- **Dates**: ~1770-1880
- **Location**: Berlin (initial center), then spread
- **Significance**: Movement for Jewish modernization, secular education, and integration into European intellectual life while maintaining Jewish identity.
- **Defining Characteristics**:
  - Hebrew language revival
  - Secular education alongside traditional learning
  - Engagement with general European philosophy
  - Push for Jewish emancipation
- **Core Members**: Moses Mendelssohn (foundational figure)
- **Relationship to General Enlightenment**: Mendelssohn bridged Jewish and German Enlightenment; argued Judaism was compatible with reason
- **Notes**: Mendelssohn called "father of the Haskalah."

### Physiocracy
- **ID**: `entity-physiocracy`
- **Type**: Economic school / intellectual movement
- **Dates**: ~1756-1780s
- **Location**: France
- **Significance**: First systematic school of economic thought. Argued land was the sole source of wealth; advocated free trade and natural economic laws.
- **Defining Characteristics**:
  - Land as basis of national wealth
  - *Tableau économique* as analytical tool
  - *Laissez-faire* economic policy
  - "Natural order" of economic laws
- **Core Members**: François Quesnay (founder), Turgot, Mirabeau (the elder)
- **Influence**: Influenced Adam Smith (critiqued but absorbed their ideas); term "laissez-faire" originated with them
- **Publications**: *Tableau économique* (1758), journal *Éphémérides du citoyen*
- **Notes**: Smith praised Quesnay highly; may have intended to dedicate *Wealth of Nations* to him.

### British Empiricism
- **ID**: `entity-british-empiricism`
- **Type**: Philosophical school
- **Dates**: ~1689-1780
- **Location**: Britain
- **Significance**: Philosophical tradition holding that knowledge derives from sensory experience rather than innate ideas or pure reason.
- **Defining Characteristics**:
  - Rejection of innate ideas
  - Experience as source of knowledge
  - Skepticism about metaphysics
  - Analysis of ideas into simple components
- **Core Members**: Locke (founder), Berkeley, Hume
- **Key Works**: Locke's *Essay*, Berkeley's *Principles*, Hume's *Treatise* and *Enquiries*
- **Influence**: Shaped Scottish Enlightenment; provoked Kant's critical philosophy
- **Notes**: Hume pushed empiricism to its skeptical conclusions; Kant responded.

### Rationalism (Continental)
- **ID**: `entity-continental-rationalism`
- **Type**: Philosophical school
- **Dates**: ~1640-1780
- **Location**: Continent (Germany, Netherlands, France)
- **Significance**: Philosophical tradition emphasizing reason over experience as source of knowledge. Major alternative to British empiricism.
- **Defining Characteristics**:
  - Innate ideas or structures
  - Reason can know reality
  - Mathematical method in philosophy
  - Systematic metaphysics
- **Core Members**: (Earlier: Descartes, Spinoza) Leibniz, Wolff
- **Influence**: Dominated German universities through Wolff; synthesized with empiricism by Kant
- **Notes**: Kant claimed to synthesize rationalism and empiricism in critical philosophy.

### Utilitarianism
- **ID**: `entity-utilitarianism`
- **Type**: Ethical/political philosophy
- **Dates**: ~1760s-onwards (foundations)
- **Location**: England, France
- **Significance**: Moral theory defining right action as that which produces greatest happiness for greatest number.
- **Defining Characteristics**:
  - Consequences determine moral value
  - Pleasure and pain as ultimate standards
  - Reformist implications for law and policy
  - Rejection of natural rights language
- **Core Members**: Bentham (systematic founder), influenced by Helvétius, Beccaria, Priestley
- **Key Works**: Helvétius's *De l'esprit*, Bentham's *Principles of Morals and Legislation*
- **Notes**: Late Enlightenment development; flowered in 19th century.

### Common Sense Philosophy
- **ID**: `entity-common-sense-philosophy`
- **Type**: Philosophical school
- **Dates**: ~1764-1850s
- **Location**: Scotland, then America
- **Significance**: Response to Hume's skepticism, arguing humans have reliable innate common sense beliefs about the world.
- **Defining Characteristics**:
  - Rejection of skeptical conclusions
  - Common sense as philosophical foundation
  - Realism about external world
  - Practical focus
- **Core Members**: Reid (founder), Stewart, later followers
- **Key Works**: Reid's *Inquiry into the Human Mind* (1764)
- **Influence**: Major influence on American college philosophy (Princeton)
- **Notes**: Alternative to both Humean skepticism and Kantian critique.

### Radical Enlightenment
- **ID**: `entity-radical-enlightenment`
- **Type**: Historiographical concept / tendency
- **Dates**: ~1680-1789
- **Location**: International
- **Significance**: Term for the most radical strands of Enlightenment thought—materialist, atheist, democratic. Distinguished from "moderate" Enlightenment.
- **Defining Characteristics**:
  - Atheism or materialist metaphysics
  - Radical democracy
  - Rejection of all traditional authority
  - Clandestine circulation of texts
- **Associated Figures**: d'Holbach, La Mettrie, (retrospectively) Spinoza as inspiration
- **Historiography**: Concept developed by Jonathan Israel to reframe Enlightenment studies
- **Notes**: Useful analytical category though debated by historians.

## 3. Formal Academies (as Entities)

### Académie française
- **ID**: `entity-academie-francaise`
- **Type**: Official academy
- **Dates**: Founded 1635; Enlightenment role 1700s
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: Guardian of French language and literature. Membership conferred prestige. Election was a major achievement for philosophes.
- **Members from Network**: Montesquieu (1728), Voltaire (1746), d'Alembert (Secretary 1772-1783)
- **Role in Network**: Conferred legitimacy; d'Alembert used position to support philosophe agenda
- **Notes**: Conservative institution captured by philosophes in mid-century.

### Académie des Sciences
- **ID**: `entity-academie-sciences`
- **Type**: Scientific academy
- **Dates**: Founded 1666
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: Leading French scientific institution. Organized research, expeditions, and publications. Platform for Enlightenment science.
- **Members from Network**: d'Alembert, Condorcet (Secretary 1776-1793), Lavoisier, Laplace, Buffon
- **Role in Network**: Institutional base for scientific members; Condorcet used secretary role for reform
- **Notes**: Permanent Secretaries wielded significant influence.

### Berlin Academy (Royal Prussian Academy of Sciences)
- **ID**: `entity-berlin-academy`
- **Type**: Scientific/philosophical academy
- **Dates**: Founded 1700 by Leibniz; revitalized 1740s
- **Location**: Berlin
- **Significance**: Major European academy under Frederick the Great. Prize competitions addressed philosophical questions. International membership.
- **Members from Network**: Euler, Lagrange, Mendelssohn (prize 1763), Kant (honorable mention)
- **Role in Network**: Connected German and French thought; prize questions shaped philosophical agenda
- **Famous Prizes**: 1763 prize on metaphysical certainty (Mendelssohn won over Kant)
- **Notes**: Frederick preferred French members; caused tension with Euler.

### Royal Society of London
- **ID**: `entity-royal-society`
- **Type**: Scientific society
- **Dates**: Founded 1660
- **Location**: London
- **Significance**: Premier English scientific society. Newton as President (1703-1727) established dominance of Newtonian natural philosophy.
- **Members from Network**: Newton (President), Locke, Franklin, Priestley
- **Role in Network**: Less philosophical than Continental academies; focused on natural philosophy
- **Notes**: Published *Philosophical Transactions*; election was international honor.

### American Philosophical Society
- **ID**: `entity-amer-phil-society`
- **Type**: Learned society
- **Dates**: Founded 1743 (reorganized 1769)
- **Location**: Philadelphia
- **Significance**: American counterpart to European academies. Franklin's creation. Focused on "useful knowledge."
- **Members from Network**: Franklin (founder), Jefferson, Adams
- **Role in Network**: Institutional expression of American Enlightenment
- **Notes**: Combined scientific and practical improvement missions.

## 4. Informal Groups and Circles

### D'Holbach's Circle (Coterie Holbachique)
- **ID**: `entity-holbach-circle`
- **Type**: Informal intellectual circle
- **Dates**: ~1750-1789
- **Location**: Paris (rue Royale, also country estate at Grandval)
- **Significance**: Most radical salon of the Enlightenment. Twice-weekly dinners produced materialist and atheist publications.
- **Core Members**: d'Holbach (host), Diderot, d'Alembert, Helvétius, visitors including Hume, Rousseau (before break)
- **Works Produced**: d'Holbach's anonymous atheist publications (*System of Nature*, etc.) discussed and refined here
- **Notes**: Rousseau's estrangement from this circle symbolized tensions between deism and atheism.

### Edinburgh Literati
- **ID**: `entity-edinburgh-literati`
- **Type**: Informal intellectual network
- **Dates**: ~1740-1800
- **Location**: Edinburgh
- **Significance**: The overlapping membership of Edinburgh's clubs, university, and professional life. Created dense network of intellectual exchange.
- **Core Members**: Hume, Smith, Ferguson, Robertson, Kames, Blair
- **Venues**: Select Society, Poker Club, taverns, private dinners
- **Notes**: Small city scale intensified interactions; everyone knew everyone.

### American Founders (as intellectual circle)
- **ID**: `entity-american-founders`
- **Type**: Political/intellectual circle
- **Dates**: ~1776-1800
- **Location**: Philadelphia, Virginia
- **Significance**: Applied Enlightenment political philosophy to constitution-making. Unique experiment in putting theory into practice.
- **Core Members**: Franklin, Jefferson, Madison, Adams
- **Key Projects**: Declaration of Independence, Constitution, Federalist Papers
- **Notes**: Combined theoretical sophistication with practical politics.

## 5. Publications and Journals (as Institutions)

### Journal des savants
- **ID**: `entity-journal-savants`
- **Type**: Academic journal
- **Dates**: Founded 1665
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: First academic journal. Reviewed books and reported scientific news. Model for scholarly communication.
- **Role in Network**: Reviewed major Enlightenment works; shaped reputation
- **Notes**: Organ of French learned establishment.

### Éphémérides du citoyen
- **ID**: `entity-ephemerides`
- **Type**: Journal
- **Dates**: 1765-1772
- **Location**: Paris
- **Significance**: Physiocrat journal. Platform for economic reform ideas.
- **Associated Figures**: Quesnay, Turgot, Mirabeau
- **Notes**: Main organ of Physiocrat school.

### Berlinische Monatsschrift
- **ID**: `entity-berlinische-monatsschrift`
- **Type**: Journal
- **Dates**: 1783-1796
- **Location**: Berlin
- **Significance**: Major German Enlightenment journal. Published Kant's "What is Enlightenment?" and Mendelssohn's companion essay.
- **Historical Note**: Question "What is Enlightenment?" was posed in its pages, answered by both Kant and Mendelssohn in 1784
- **Notes**: Shows German Aufklärung at its height.

## 6. Reform Movements and Causes

### Religious Toleration Movement
- **ID**: `entity-religious-toleration`
- **Type**: Reform movement
- **Dates**: Throughout
- **Significance**: Central Enlightenment cause. Argued for freedom of worship, end of persecution, separation of church and state.
- **Key Advocates**: Voltaire (Calas affair), Locke (*Letter Concerning Toleration*), Mendelssohn (*Jerusalem*), Lessing (*Nathan the Wise*)
- **Achievements**: Some legal reforms; intellectual shift toward toleration as norm
- **Notes**: Voltaire's campaigns against religious persecution defined his later career.

### Criminal Justice Reform
- **ID**: `entity-criminal-justice-reform`
- **Type**: Reform movement
- **Dates**: 1760s-1800
- **Significance**: Movement to abolish torture, reduce death penalty, make punishment proportional to crime.
- **Key Advocates**: Beccaria (*On Crimes and Punishments*), Voltaire (commentary on Beccaria), Bentham
- **Achievements**: Influenced penal codes across Europe and America
- **Notes**: Beccaria's work translated into every major European language.

### Enlightened Despotism
- **ID**: `entity-enlightened-despotism`
- **Type**: Political phenomenon / ideology
- **Dates**: ~1740-1790
- **Location**: Prussia, Russia, Austria, and elsewhere
- **Significance**: Alliance between absolutist monarchs and Enlightenment ideas. Rulers claimed to govern according to reason for the good of their subjects.
- **Key Figures**: Frederick the Great (Prussia), Catherine the Great (Russia), Joseph II (Austria)
- **Relationship to Philosophes**: Frederick and Catherine corresponded with Voltaire, hosted philosophes, patronized academies
- **Critique**: Philosophes debated whether reform from above was authentic or merely instrumental
- **Notes**: Showed both possibilities and limits of Enlightenment political reform.

## 7. Religious and Anti-Religious Currents

### Deism
- **ID**: `entity-deism`
- **Type**: Religious/philosophical position
- **Dates**: ~1690-1800
- **Location**: International
- **Significance**: "Natural religion" based on reason rather than revelation. Accepted God as creator but rejected miracles, prophecy, and specific doctrines.
- **Defining Characteristics**:
  - God known through reason and nature
  - Rejection of revealed religion
  - Moral emphasis
  - Often anti-clerical
- **Key Advocates**: Voltaire, Paine (*Age of Reason*), Rousseau
- **Contrast**: Distinguished from atheism (d'Holbach) and traditional Christianity
- **Notes**: Mainstream Enlightenment religious position; Rousseau's deism caused break with atheist philosophes.

### Enlightenment Atheism
- **ID**: `entity-enlightenment-atheism`
- **Type**: Philosophical/religious position
- **Dates**: ~1740-1790
- **Location**: Primarily France
- **Significance**: Explicit rejection of God's existence. Rare but influential. Associated with materialism.
- **Key Advocates**: d'Holbach (*System of Nature*), La Mettrie, Diderot (in private writings)
- **Key Works**: *System of Nature* (1770), *L'Homme Machine* (1747)
- **Notes**: Dangerous position; most atheist works published anonymously. Hume's *Dialogues* approached atheism.

### Moderate Enlightenment (Church)
- **ID**: `entity-moderate-enlightenment`
- **Type**: Position/tendency
- **Dates**: Throughout
- **Location**: International
- **Significance**: Attempts to reconcile Christianity with Enlightenment values. Reform-minded clergy who accepted reason and criticism.
- **Examples**: Church of Scotland "Moderates" (led by Robertson), some Catholic reformers
- **Notes**: Shows Enlightenment was not uniformly anti-religious.

## 8. Political Entities Shaped by Enlightenment

### United States of America
- **ID**: `entity-united-states`
- **Type**: Nation-state
- **Dates**: 1776-present (founding period focus)
- **Significance**: First nation explicitly founded on Enlightenment principles. Declaration of Independence, Constitution, and Bill of Rights expressed Enlightenment political philosophy.
- **Enlightenment Founders**: Franklin, Jefferson, Madison, Adams
- **Key Texts**: Declaration of Independence (Lockean natural rights), Constitution (Montesquieu's separation of powers), Federalist Papers
- **Notes**: "Novus ordo seclorum" (new order of the ages)—self-consciously Enlightenment experiment.

### French Revolution (as transformation)
- **ID**: `entity-french-revolution`
- **Type**: Political event/transformation
- **Dates**: 1789-1799
- **Location**: France
- **Significance**: Attempted to put Enlightenment ideas into practice. Philosophes were dead by 1789 but their ideas shaped revolutionaries.
- **Enlightenment Legacy**: Declaration of the Rights of Man (1789), anti-clerical legislation, republican ideology
- **Victims from Network**: Lavoisier (guillotined), Condorcet (died in prison)
- **Debate**: Did Enlightenment cause the Revolution? Burke blamed it; historians debate.
- **Notes**: Shows both fulfillment and corruption of Enlightenment ideals.

## Notes on Entity Relationships

1. **Overlapping memberships**: Many individuals belonged to multiple entities (e.g., Diderot: philosophes, Encyclopédie project, d'Holbach's circle)

2. **Entity evolution**: Some entities evolved over time (e.g., Encyclopédie project shifted after d'Alembert's departure)

3. **Tensions between entities**: Key conflicts shaped network (e.g., Rousseau's break with philosophes; deism vs. atheism)

4. **Institutional vs. informal**: Formal academies provided stability; informal circles enabled radical thought

5. **National variations**: French philosophes, Scottish Enlightenment, and German Aufklärung had distinct characters while sharing underlying commitments
