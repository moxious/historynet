## Church Bodies

### Lutheran Churches (Territorial)
- **ID**: `entity-lutheran-churches`
- **Title**: Lutheran Territorial Churches
- **Type**: church_body
- **Date Range**: 1520s-present
- **Headquarters**: Various (Saxony, Prussia, Denmark, Sweden, etc.)
- **Significance**: Network of state churches that adopted Lutheran confession. Each territory organized its own church under princely authority. Model of cuius regio, eius religio (whose realm, his religion).
- **Key Features**: Territorial organization; princely authority over church; Augsburg Confession as standard; episcopal or consistorial governance
- **Key Members**: Electoral Saxony (Luther's base), Ducal Prussia, Brandenburg, Denmark-Norway, Sweden
- **Evidence**: Church orders (Kirchenordnungen) from various territories survive

### Church of England
- **ID**: `entity-church-of-england`
- **Title**: Church of England (Ecclesia Anglicana)
- **Type**: church_body
- **Date Range**: 1534-present
- **Headquarters**: Canterbury
- **Founded By**: Henry VIII (royal supremacy), Cranmer (liturgical/doctrinal)
- **Significance**: National Protestant church established by royal supremacy. Unique blend of Catholic structure (episcopacy) with Protestant theology. Book of Common Prayer unified liturgy.
- **Key Features**: Episcopal governance; royal supremacy; Prayer Book liturgy; Thirty-Nine Articles
- **Key Members/Leaders**: Henry VIII (supreme head), Cranmer (architect), Parker (consolidator)
- **Evidence**: Acts of Parliament; Prayer Books; Thirty-Nine Articles

### Reformed Church of Zurich
- **ID**: `entity-zurich-church`
- **Title**: Reformed Church of Zurich
- **Type**: church_body
- **Date Range**: 1519-present
- **Headquarters**: location-zurich
- **Founded By**: Zwingli (with city council)
- **Significance**: First Reformed church. Model for Swiss Reformed organization. Antistes (head pastor) as leading minister. City council role in church government.
- **Key Features**: Antistes leadership; city council authority; Zwinglian theology
- **Key Members/Leaders**: Zwingli (founder), Bullinger (successor Antistes)
- **Evidence**: Zurich church records; council minutes

### Reformed Church of Geneva
- **ID**: `entity-geneva-church`
- **Title**: Genevan Reformed Church
- **Type**: church_body
- **Date Range**: 1536-present
- **Headquarters**: location-geneva
- **Founded By**: Farel, Calvin
- **Significance**: Model for Calvinist church organization worldwide. Consistory for discipline. Company of Pastors for ministry. Influenced Presbyterian governance globally.
- **Key Features**: Consistory (discipline); Company of Pastors; Geneva Academy (training); fourfold ministry (pastors, teachers, elders, deacons)
- **Key Members/Leaders**: Calvin, Farel, Beza, Company of Pastors
- **Evidence**: Ecclesiastical Ordinances (1541); Consistory records survive

### Company of Pastors (Geneva)
- **ID**: `entity-company-of-pastors`
- **Title**: Vénérable Compagnie des Pasteurs
- **Type**: organization
- **Date Range**: 1541-present
- **Headquarters**: location-geneva
- **Founded By**: Calvin (Ecclesiastical Ordinances)
- **Significance**: Collective body of Geneva's pastors meeting weekly for Scripture interpretation and mutual accountability. Model for Presbyterian minister courts.
- **Key Features**: Weekly meetings; collective biblical interpretation; ministerial accountability
- **Key Members/Leaders**: Calvin (moderator), Farel, Beza, Geneva pastors
- **Evidence**: Company records survive from 1546

### Consistory of Geneva
- **ID**: `entity-geneva-consistory`
- **Title**: Geneva Consistory
- **Type**: organization
- **Date Range**: 1541-present
- **Headquarters**: location-geneva
- **Founded By**: Calvin (Ecclesiastical Ordinances)
- **Significance**: Disciplinary body for Genevan church. Pastors and elders met weekly to enforce moral discipline. Model for Reformed church discipline worldwide.
- **Key Features**: Elders and pastors; moral oversight; excommunication power (after 1555)
- **Key Members/Leaders**: Calvin (leading pastor), city elders
- **Evidence**: Extensive Consistory records survive; studied by historians

### Church of Scotland (Kirk)
- **ID**: `entity-church-of-scotland`
- **Title**: Church of Scotland (The Kirk)
- **Type**: church_body
- **Date Range**: 1560-present
- **Headquarters**: Edinburgh (St. Giles)
- **Founded By**: John Knox and others
- **Significance**: National Reformed church of Scotland. Presbyterian governance with kirk sessions, presbyteries, synods, and General Assembly. Influenced by Geneva but distinctly Scottish.
- **Key Features**: Presbyterian governance; Scots Confession; Book of Discipline; General Assembly
- **Key Members/Leaders**: Knox (founder), Scottish Reformers
- **Evidence**: Acts of Parliament (1560); First Book of Discipline; General Assembly records

## Movements

### Lutheran Reformation
- **ID**: `entity-lutheran-reformation`
- **Title**: Lutheran Reformation
- **Type**: movement
- **Date Range**: 1517-1555 (initial phase)
- **Headquarters**: location-wittenberg
- **Significance**: The initial Protestant movement sparked by Luther's Ninety-Five Theses (1517). Centered on justification by faith alone, Scripture alone, and critique of papal authority.
- **Key Doctrines**: Sola fide, sola scriptura, priesthood of all believers, two kingdoms
- **Key Figures**: Luther, Melanchthon, Bugenhagen, Jonas, Lutheran princes
- **Evidence**: Luther's writings; Augsburg Confession; spread across Germany and Scandinavia

### Swiss Reformed Movement
- **ID**: `entity-swiss-reformed`
- **Title**: Swiss Reformed Movement
- **Type**: movement
- **Date Range**: 1519-1566
- **Headquarters**: location-zurich, location-geneva
- **Significance**: Reformed Protestantism originating independently of Luther in Zurich (Zwingli) and later systematized in Geneva (Calvin). Distinctive theology on sacraments, predestination, and church governance.
- **Key Doctrines**: Symbolic Eucharist view; predestination; covenantal theology; church discipline
- **Key Figures**: Zwingli, Bullinger, Calvin, Farel, Beza
- **Evidence**: Zwingli's writings; Calvin's Institutes; Reformed confessions

### Radical Reformation
- **ID**: `entity-radical-reformation`
- **Title**: Radical Reformation
- **Type**: movement
- **Date Range**: 1525-1560s
- **Significance**: Umbrella term for reform movements that rejected both Catholic and magisterial Protestant churches. Emphasized adult baptism, separation from world, pacifism (mostly), and voluntary church membership.
- **Key Streams**: Swiss Brethren (Anabaptists), South German Anabaptists, Hutterites, Mennonites, Spiritualists
- **Key Figures**: Grebel, Manz, Hubmaier, Menno Simons, Sattler
- **Evidence**: Schleitheim Confession; Anabaptist martyrologies; Menno's writings

### Swiss Brethren
- **ID**: `entity-swiss-brethren`
- **Title**: Swiss Brethren (Täufer)
- **Type**: organization/movement
- **Date Range**: 1525-present (as Mennonites/Amish)
- **Headquarters**: Initially Zurich
- **Founded By**: Conrad Grebel, Felix Manz
- **Significance**: First Anabaptist group. Broke with Zwingli over infant baptism (1525). First adult baptisms January 21, 1525. Severely persecuted by both Catholics and magisterial Protestants.
- **Key Doctrines**: Believers' baptism; separation from world; nonresistance; church discipline (ban)
- **Key Figures**: Grebel, Manz, Blaurock, Sattler
- **Evidence**: Schleitheim Confession; trial records; martyrologies

### Mennonites
- **ID**: `entity-mennonites`
- **Title**: Mennonite Movement
- **Type**: movement/church_body
- **Date Range**: 1536-present
- **Founded By**: Menno Simons (reorganized Dutch Anabaptism)
- **Significance**: Peaceful Anabaptist movement that survived persecution. Named after Menno Simons who reorganized scattered Dutch Anabaptists after Münster catastrophe (1534-35). Emphasized nonviolence and discipleship.
- **Key Doctrines**: Nonresistance; believers' baptism; simple life; church discipline
- **Key Figures**: Menno Simons
- **Evidence**: Menno's writings; Mennonite communities survive

### Christian Humanism
- **ID**: `entity-christian-humanism`
- **Title**: Christian Humanism
- **Type**: movement
- **Date Range**: 1490s-1540s
- **Significance**: Intellectual movement emphasizing return to biblical and patristic sources (ad fontes), Greek and Hebrew scholarship, educational reform, and moral renewal. Not itself Protestant but provided methods and criticisms that reformers used.
- **Key Features**: Biblical scholarship; classical learning; critique of scholasticism; call for church reform
- **Key Figures**: Erasmus, Colet, More (until break), Reuchlin, Lefèvre d'Étaples
- **Evidence**: Erasmus's editions and writings; humanist correspondence networks

### English Reformation
- **ID**: `entity-english-reformation`
- **Title**: English Reformation
- **Type**: movement
- **Date Range**: 1527-1559 (initial phase)
- **Headquarters**: London, Canterbury
- **Significance**: Reformation in England driven by both political (Henry's divorce) and religious factors. Unique in maintaining episcopacy while adopting Protestant theology. Multiple phases under Henry, Edward, Mary, Elizabeth.
- **Key Features**: Royal supremacy; Prayer Book liturgy; episcopal governance; via media (middle way)
- **Key Figures**: Henry VIII, Cranmer, Cromwell, Latimer, Ridley, Parker
- **Evidence**: Acts of Parliament; Prayer Books; English Bible; martyrdom accounts

### Meaux Circle
- **ID**: `entity-meaux-circle`
- **Title**: Meaux Circle (Cercle de Meaux)
- **Type**: movement
- **Date Range**: 1521-1525
- **Headquarters**: location-meaux
- **Founded By**: Guillaume Briçonnet (bishop)
- **Significance**: Early French evangelical reform movement under Bishop Briçonnet's patronage. Included Lefèvre d'Étaples and young Guillaume Farel. Suppressed by Sorbonne pressure after 1525.
- **Key Features**: Reform within Catholic structure; biblical preaching; Lefèvre's scholarship
- **Key Figures**: Briçonnet, Lefèvre d'Étaples, Farel (briefly), Marguerite of Navarre (protector)
- **Evidence**: Briçonnet's correspondence; diocese records; Sorbonne condemnations

## Political Alliances

### Schmalkaldic League
- **ID**: `entity-schmalkaldic-league`
- **Title**: Schmalkaldic League (Schmalkaldischer Bund)
- **Type**: political_alliance
- **Date Range**: 1531-1547
- **Headquarters**: Schmalkalden (Hesse)
- **Founded By**: John of Saxony, Philip of Hesse
- **Significance**: Military and political alliance of Lutheran princes and cities against Emperor Charles V. First Protestant military coalition. Defeated in Schmalkaldic War (1546-47) but won religious peace at Augsburg (1555).
- **Key Features**: Mutual defense; Protestant solidarity; Lutheran confession basis
- **Key Members**: Electoral Saxony, Hesse, numerous cities and territories
- **Key Figures**: John the Steadfast, John Frederick I, Philip of Hesse
- **Evidence**: League records; Peace of Augsburg (1555) resulted from their resistance
- **Key Events**:
  - 1531: League formed at Schmalkalden
  - 1546-47: Schmalkaldic War; League defeated
  - 1547: John Frederick captured at Mühlberg
  - 1552: Protestant uprising forces Charles V to negotiate
  - 1555: Peace of Augsburg

### Swiss Confederacy
- **ID**: `entity-swiss-confederacy`
- **Title**: Swiss Confederacy
- **Type**: political_alliance
- **Date Range**: 1291-present
- **Headquarters**: None (federation)
- **Significance**: Federation of Swiss cantons. Reformation divided confederacy between Reformed (Zurich, Bern, Basel, Geneva) and Catholic (central cantons) cantons. Two Kappel wars (1529, 1531) over religion.
- **Key Features**: Cantonal autonomy; confessional division; Kappel peace
- **Key Members**: Reformed: Zurich, Bern, Basel; Catholic: Uri, Schwyz, others
- **Evidence**: Confederation records; Kappel peace treaties

## Religious Orders

### Augustinian Order (Augustinian Hermits)
- **ID**: `entity-augustinian-order`
- **Title**: Order of Augustinian Hermits
- **Type**: religious_order
- **Date Range**: 1244-present
- **Significance**: Luther's religious order. Entered Augustinian friary at Erfurt (1505); ordained priest (1507). Influenced by Augustinian theology of grace. Johann von Staupitz was Luther's superior and mentor.
- **Key Features**: Augustinian spirituality; emphasis on grace; Luther's formation
- **Key Figures**: Luther (member), Johann von Staupitz (vicar general, Luther's mentor)
- **Evidence**: Augustinian records; Luther's own accounts

### Dominican Order
- **ID**: `entity-dominican-order`
- **Title**: Order of Preachers (Dominicans)
- **Type**: religious_order
- **Date Range**: 1216-present
- **Significance**: Order of Johann Tetzel (indulgence preacher) and Cardinal Cajetan (who interrogated Luther). Traditionally associated with Inquisition and theological education. Often Catholic opponents of reform.
- **Key Features**: Theological education; preaching; Inquisition role
- **Key Figures**: Johann Tetzel, Cardinal Cajetan
- **Evidence**: Dominican records; polemical literature

## Councils, Diets, and Assemblies

### Diet of Worms (1521)
- **ID**: `entity-diet-of-worms`
- **Title**: Diet of Worms (1521)
- **Type**: assembly
- **Date**: January-May 1521
- **Location**: location-worms
- **Significance**: Imperial diet where Luther appeared before Emperor Charles V and refused to recant. "Here I stand" moment. Resulted in Edict of Worms declaring Luther outlaw.
- **Key Events**:
  - April 17-18, 1521: Luther's appearance and refusal to recant
  - May 25, 1521: Edict of Worms
- **Key Figures**: Luther, Charles V, Frederick the Wise, papal legates
- **Evidence**: Diet records; Edict text survives; accounts of Luther's appearance

### Diet of Augsburg (1530)
- **ID**: `entity-diet-of-augsburg`
- **Title**: Diet of Augsburg (1530)
- **Type**: assembly
- **Date**: June-November 1530
- **Location**: location-augsburg
- **Significance**: Imperial diet where Augsburg Confession was presented (June 25). Charles V sought religious unity. Catholics presented Confutatio in response. Failed to achieve reconciliation; led to Schmalkaldic League formation.
- **Key Events**:
  - June 25, 1530: Augsburg Confession read
  - July 1530: Tetrapolitan Confession also presented
  - August 1530: Catholic Confutatio
- **Key Figures**: Charles V, Melanchthon (author of Confession), Eck (Confutatio)
- **Evidence**: Confession and Confutatio texts survive; diet records

### Marburg Colloquy (1529)
- **ID**: `entity-marburg-colloquy`
- **Title**: Marburg Colloquy
- **Type**: assembly
- **Date**: October 1-4, 1529
- **Location**: location-marburg
- **Hosted By**: Philip of Hesse
- **Significance**: Attempt to unite Lutheran and Zwinglian reformers. Agreed on 14 of 15 articles but failed on Eucharist. Luther's "Hoc est corpus meum" vs. Zwingli's symbolic interpretation. Prevented Protestant political unity.
- **Key Debate**: Real presence (Luther) vs. symbolic presence (Zwingli)
- **Key Figures**: Luther, Zwingli, Melanchthon, Bucer, Oecolampadius, Philip of Hesse
- **Evidence**: Marburg Articles text; accounts by participants

### First Zurich Disputation (1523)
- **ID**: `entity-zurich-disputation-1523`
- **Title**: First Zurich Disputation
- **Type**: assembly
- **Date**: January 29, 1523
- **Location**: location-zurich
- **Significance**: Public disputation organized by Zurich city council. Zwingli defended his Sixty-Seven Articles. Council ruled in his favor, establishing legal basis for Zurich Reformation.
- **Key Outcome**: City council supports Zwingli; Reformation begins officially
- **Key Figures**: Zwingli, Johannes Fabri (Catholic opponent), Zurich city council
- **Evidence**: Disputation records; council decision

### Second Zurich Disputation (1523)
- **ID**: `entity-zurich-disputation-oct-1523`
- **Title**: Second Zurich Disputation
- **Type**: assembly
- **Date**: October 26-28, 1523
- **Location**: location-zurich
- **Significance**: Debated images and the mass. More radical outcome—eventually led to removal of images and abolition of mass. Also surfaced tensions with future Anabaptists (Grebel, Manz) who wanted faster reform.
- **Key Debates**: Images; Mass; pace of reform
- **Key Figures**: Zwingli, Grebel, Manz, city council
- **Evidence**: Disputation records; accounts of image removal that followed

### Leipzig Debate (1519)
- **ID**: `entity-leipzig-debate`
- **Title**: Leipzig Debate
- **Type**: assembly
- **Date**: June-July 1519
- **Location**: Leipzig
- **Significance**: Academic debate between Luther/Karlstadt and Johann Eck. Eck maneuvered Luther into denying papal primacy and admitting sympathy with some of Hus's views. Accelerated Luther's break with Rome.
- **Key Outcomes**: Luther denies papal primacy; admits councils can err; Eck claims victory
- **Key Figures**: Luther, Karlstadt, Eck
- **Evidence**: Debate transcripts; contemporary accounts

### Peace of Augsburg (1555)
- **ID**: `entity-peace-of-augsburg`
- **Title**: Peace of Augsburg
- **Type**: treaty
- **Date**: September 25, 1555
- **Location**: location-augsburg
- **Significance**: Imperial settlement establishing legal coexistence of Lutheranism and Catholicism in Holy Roman Empire. Cuius regio, eius religio principle: ruler determines religion of territory. Excluded Reformed and Anabaptists.
- **Key Principles**: Cuius regio, eius religio; ecclesiastical reservation; status quo for imperial cities
- **Key Figures**: Charles V (abdicated soon after), Ferdinand I, Lutheran and Catholic princes
- **Evidence**: Treaty text survives; foundational for religious settlement until Thirty Years War

## Publishing Networks

### Froben Press (Basel)
- **ID**: `entity-froben-press`
- **Title**: Froben Press (Officina Frobeniana)
- **Type**: publishing_house
- **Date Range**: 1491-1527
- **Location**: location-basel
- **Founded By**: Johann Froben
- **Significance**: Premier humanist publishing house. Published Erasmus's works including Greek New Testament (1516). High-quality scholarly editions. Erasmus lived with Froben in Basel.
- **Key Publications**: Erasmus's Greek NT (1516); Erasmus's collected works; Church Fathers editions
- **Key Figures**: Johann Froben (printer), Erasmus (author), Hans Holbein (illustrations)
- **Evidence**: Many Froben editions survive; recognizable typography and quality

### Wittenberg Printers
- **ID**: `entity-wittenberg-printers`
- **Title**: Wittenberg Printing Network
- **Type**: publishing_network
- **Date Range**: 1517-1560s
- **Location**: location-wittenberg
- **Significance**: Network of printers who published Luther's works and Reformation literature. Made Wittenberg major publishing center. Rapid dissemination of Luther's works across Europe.
- **Key Printers**: Melchior Lotter, Hans Lufft (Luther's Bible printer), Georg Rhau
- **Key Publications**: Luther's treatises, German Bible, hymnals, catechisms
- **Evidence**: Numerous surviving editions with Wittenberg imprints

### Geneva Printers
- **ID**: `entity-geneva-printers`
- **Title**: Geneva Printing Network
- **Type**: publishing_network
- **Date Range**: 1540s-1600s
- **Location**: location-geneva
- **Significance**: Published Calvin's works, Geneva Bible (1560), Genevan Psalter, and Reformed literature. Geneva became major Reformed publishing center, distributing books across Europe.
- **Key Printers**: Jean Girard, Robert Estienne, Conrad Badius
- **Key Publications**: Institutes, Geneva Bible, Genevan Psalter, Reformed pamphlets
- **Evidence**: Geneva imprints survive; censorship records show feared influence
