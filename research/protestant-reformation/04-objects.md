**Date**: 2026-01-18
**Last Updated**: 2026-01-18
**Purpose**: Identify key works, publications, and documents for the Protestant Reformation network.

1. **Theological Treatises**: Major doctrinal works

## Theological Treatises

### Ninety-Five Theses (1517)
- **ID**: `object-ninety-five-theses`
- **Title**: Ninety-Five Theses (Disputatio pro declaratione virtutis indulgentiarum)
- **Creator**: person-martin-luther
- **Date**: October 31, 1517
- **Type**: treatise
- **Language**: Latin
- **Significance**: The document that sparked the Reformation. Luther's academic disputation challenging the sale of indulgences and papal authority over purgatory. Rapidly disseminated via printing press across Europe.
- **Key Arguments**: Critiqued indulgence selling, asserted pope cannot release souls from purgatory, questioned treasury of merits
- **Connections**: Responded to Johann Tetzel's indulgence sales; triggered papal investigation; led to Leipzig Debate
- **Evidence**: Original broadside printed in Wittenberg; multiple 1517 printings survive

### Address to the Christian Nobility of the German Nation (1520)
- **ID**: `object-address-christian-nobility`
- **Title**: An den christlichen Adel deutscher Nation von des christlichen Standes Besserung
- **Creator**: person-martin-luther
- **Date**: August 1520
- **Type**: treatise
- **Language**: German
- **Significance**: First of Luther's 1520 "Reformation treatises." Called on German princes to reform the church, attacked papal authority, advocated for clergy marriage and national church reform.
- **Key Arguments**: Three "walls" of papal authority must fall; secular authorities have duty to reform church; priesthood of all believers
- **Connections**: Addressed to Charles V and German princes; contributed to Exsurge Domine bull
- **Evidence**: Published in Wittenberg; 4,000 copies sold in first week

### On the Babylonian Captivity of the Church (1520)
- **ID**: `object-babylonian-captivity`
- **Title**: De captivitate Babylonica ecclesiae praeludium
- **Creator**: person-martin-luther
- **Date**: October 1520
- **Type**: treatise
- **Language**: Latin
- **Significance**: Luther's most radical theological work of 1520. Attacked sacramental system, reducing seven sacraments to three (later two). Made reconciliation with Rome impossible.
- **Key Arguments**: Only baptism, Eucharist, and penance are valid sacraments; transubstantiation rejected; papal "captivity" of the church
- **Connections**: Erasmus called it "irremediable"; contributed to Luther's excommunication
- **Evidence**: Published October 1520; widely reprinted

### On the Freedom of a Christian (1520)
- **ID**: `object-freedom-christian`
- **Title**: Von der Freiheit eines Christenmenschen / De libertate christiana
- **Creator**: person-martin-luther
- **Date**: November 1520
- **Type**: treatise
- **Language**: German and Latin
- **Significance**: Luther's clearest statement of justification by faith alone. Balanced spiritual freedom with duty to neighbor. Accompanied conciliatory letter to Pope Leo X.
- **Key Arguments**: Christian is free through faith; Christian is servant to neighbor through love; faith alone justifies
- **Connections**: Sent with letter to Leo X; influential on Protestant ethics
- **Evidence**: Published in both German and Latin versions, November 1520

### The Bondage of the Will (1525)
- **ID**: `object-bondage-of-will`
- **Title**: De servo arbitrio
- **Creator**: person-martin-luther
- **Date**: December 1525
- **Type**: treatise
- **Language**: Latin
- **Significance**: Luther's response to Erasmus's On Free Will. Systematic statement of Luther's understanding of grace, predestination, and human inability. Marked definitive break between Luther and Erasmus.
- **Key Arguments**: Human will is bound by sin; salvation entirely by grace; God's sovereignty in salvation
- **Connections**: Responds to object-free-will-erasmus; end of Luther-Erasmus alliance
- **Evidence**: Published December 1525; Luther later called it one of his best works

### On Free Will (1524)
- **ID**: `object-free-will-erasmus`
- **Title**: De libero arbitrio diatribe sive collatio
- **Creator**: person-erasmus
- **Date**: September 1524
- **Type**: treatise
- **Language**: Latin
- **Significance**: Erasmus's critique of Luther's position on human will. Argued for human cooperation with grace. Represented humanist objection to Protestant determinism.
- **Key Arguments**: Scripture ambiguous on predestination; humans have limited free will; moderation in theological disputes preferable
- **Connections**: Provoked Luther's Bondage of the Will; marked Erasmus's break with Reformation
- **Evidence**: Published September 1524; commissioned by Catholic authorities

### Institutes of the Christian Religion (1536-1559)
- **ID**: `object-institutes`
- **Title**: Institutio Christianae Religionis
- **Creator**: person-john-calvin
- **Date**: 1536 (first edition); 1559 (final Latin edition)
- **Type**: treatise
- **Language**: Latin (also French translation)
- **Significance**: The definitive systematic theology of Reformed Protestantism. Grew from brief catechism (1536) to comprehensive theological treatise (1559). Shaped Reformed churches worldwide.
- **Key Arguments**: Systematic presentation of Protestant theology; double predestination; church order and discipline; covenant theology
- **Connections**: Dedicated to Francis I of France; influenced Beza, Knox, and all Reformed tradition
- **Evidence**: Multiple editions: 1536 (6 chapters), 1541, 1543, 1550, 1559 (80 chapters in 4 books)

### Sixty-Seven Articles (1523)
- **ID**: `object-sixty-seven-articles`
- **Title**: Schlussreden / Conclusiones
- **Creator**: person-huldrych-zwingli
- **Date**: January 1523
- **Type**: treatise
- **Language**: German
- **Significance**: Zwingli's platform for the First Zurich Disputation. Established basis for Swiss Reformed theology, distinct from Lutheran approach.
- **Key Arguments**: Scripture as sole authority; Christ as sole mediator; critique of mass, images, clerical celibacy
- **Connections**: Basis for First Zurich Disputation; led to Zurich's official adoption of Reformation
- **Evidence**: Published January 1523; debated before Zurich city council

### Commentary on True and False Religion (1525)
- **ID**: `object-true-false-religion`
- **Title**: De vera et falsa religione commentarius
- **Creator**: person-huldrych-zwingli
- **Date**: March 1525
- **Type**: treatise
- **Language**: Latin
- **Significance**: Zwingli's most comprehensive theological work. Systematic presentation of his distinctive theology including symbolic view of Eucharist.
- **Key Arguments**: Symbolic/memorial view of Lord's Supper; distinction between inner and outer religion; Scripture authority
- **Connections**: Dedicated to Francis I; distinguished Swiss from Lutheran position on Eucharist
- **Evidence**: Published March 1525; major Reformed theological text

### Loci Communes (1521)
- **ID**: `object-loci-communes`
- **Title**: Loci communes rerum theologicarum
- **Creator**: person-philip-melanchthon
- **Date**: 1521 (many revisions through 1559)
- **Type**: treatise
- **Language**: Latin
- **Significance**: First systematic presentation of Lutheran theology. Organized Protestant thought around key doctrinal "places" (loci). Luther called it worthy of the biblical canon.
- **Key Arguments**: Systematic treatment of sin, law, grace, justification; influenced by humanist method
- **Connections**: Basis for Lutheran theological education; influenced by Luther, revised toward more mediating positions in later editions
- **Evidence**: 1521 first edition; numerous revised editions; widely used theological textbook

### The Obedience of a Christian Man (1528)
- **ID**: `object-obedience-christian-man`
- **Title**: The Obedience of a Christian Man and How Christian Rulers Ought to Govern
- **Creator**: person-william-tyndale
- **Date**: 1528
- **Type**: treatise
- **Language**: English
- **Significance**: Tyndale's argument for royal supremacy over church. Reportedly shown to Henry VIII by Anne Boleyn, influencing royal supremacy doctrine.
- **Key Arguments**: Scripture in vernacular essential; king supreme over clergy; obedience to Scripture, then to civil authority
- **Connections**: Influenced Henry VIII's thinking on royal supremacy; written in exile in Antwerp
- **Evidence**: Published in Antwerp 1528; Anne Boleyn allegedly showed it to Henry VIII

## Bible Translations

### Luther's German Bible
- **ID**: `object-luther-bible`
- **Title**: Biblia / Die gantze Heilige Schrifft Deudsch
- **Creator**: person-martin-luther (with Melanchthon, Bugenhagen, Jonas, and Cruciger)
- **Date**: NT 1522 ("September Testament"); complete Bible 1534
- **Type**: translation
- **Language**: German
- **Significance**: Most influential German Bible translation. Established modern literary German. Translation from original Hebrew and Greek. Continued to be revised throughout Luther's life.
- **Translation Philosophy**: Idiomatic German; sense-for-sense translation; Lutheran theological interpretations (e.g., "allein" in Romans 3:28)
- **Connections**: Based on Erasmus's Greek NT; produced at Wartburg Castle and Wittenberg; Melanchthon assisted with Greek, others with Hebrew
- **Evidence**: September Testament (1522) sold 5,000 copies in two months; complete Bible (1534) published by Hans Lufft

### Erasmus's Greek New Testament (1516)
- **ID**: `object-novum-instrumentum`
- **Title**: Novum Instrumentum omne (later Novum Testamentum)
- **Creator**: person-erasmus
- **Date**: 1516 (five editions through 1535)
- **Type**: translation/critical text
- **Language**: Greek with Latin translation
- **Significance**: First published Greek New Testament. Made possible vernacular translations from original language. Included annotations that inspired reform.
- **Scholarly Approach**: Parallel Greek and Latin columns; extensive annotations; critical of Vulgate errors
- **Connections**: Used by Luther, Tyndale, and virtually all Protestant translators; challenged Vulgate authority
- **Evidence**: Published by Froben in Basel, 1516; multiple editions with revisions

### Tyndale's New Testament (1526)
- **ID**: `object-tyndale-nt`
- **Title**: The Newe Testament
- **Creator**: person-william-tyndale
- **Date**: 1526 (revised 1534)
- **Type**: translation
- **Language**: English
- **Significance**: First printed English New Testament translated from Greek. Approximately 80% of its wording appears in the King James Bible. Shaped English biblical language.
- **Translation Philosophy**: Translated from Greek (Erasmus's text); idiomatic English; Protestant terminology (e.g., "congregation" instead of "church")
- **Connections**: Based on Erasmus's Greek NT; printed in Worms and Cologne; smuggled into England; Coverdale continued the work
- **Evidence**: First complete edition (Worms, 1526); most copies destroyed; revised 1534

### Tyndale's Pentateuch (1530)
- **ID**: `object-tyndale-pentateuch`
- **Title**: The Firste Boke of Moses called Genesis...
- **Creator**: person-william-tyndale
- **Date**: 1530
- **Type**: translation
- **Language**: English
- **Significance**: First printed English translation of Old Testament books from Hebrew. Tyndale's Hebrew work was cut short by his execution.
- **Translation Philosophy**: From Hebrew; vivid English prose
- **Connections**: Continued by Coverdale after Tyndale's death; incorporated into later English Bibles
- **Evidence**: Published in Antwerp, 1530

### Coverdale Bible (1535)
- **ID**: `object-coverdale-bible`
- **Title**: Biblia: The Byble
- **Creator**: person-miles-coverdale
- **Date**: 1535
- **Type**: translation
- **Language**: English
- **Significance**: First complete printed English Bible. Coverdale used Tyndale's work where available and translated rest from German and Latin (not Hebrew directly).
- **Translation Philosophy**: Compiled from Tyndale, Luther, Vulgate, and other sources
- **Connections**: Incorporated Tyndale's NT and Pentateuch; basis for later English Bibles; dedicated to Henry VIII
- **Evidence**: Published 1535, probably in Cologne or Marburg; first Bible legally distributed in England

### Great Bible (1539)
- **ID**: `object-great-bible`
- **Title**: The Byble in Englyshe
- **Creator**: person-miles-coverdale (editor/reviser)
- **Date**: 1539
- **Type**: translation
- **Language**: English
- **Significance**: First officially authorized English Bible. Ordered placed in every parish church. Based on Coverdale's work, revised with Tyndale's later translations.
- **Official Status**: Authorized by Henry VIII; preface by Cranmer; placed in parish churches
- **Connections**: Thomas Cromwell commissioned it; title page by Holbein; Coverdale edited
- **Evidence**: Published by Richard Grafton and Edward Whitchurch, 1539; multiple editions

### Geneva Bible (1560)
- **ID**: `object-geneva-bible`
- **Title**: The Bible and Holy Scriptures
- **Creator**: William Whittingham, Anthony Gilby, Thomas Sampson, and others
- **Date**: 1560
- **Type**: translation
- **Language**: English
- **Significance**: Produced by Marian exiles in Geneva. First English Bible with verse numbers. Extensive marginal notes with Calvinist commentary. Most popular English Bible until KJV.
- **Features**: Verse divisions; extensive annotations; clear Roman type; portable size
- **Connections**: Produced by exiles during Mary I's reign; influenced by Beza and Calvin; Shakespeare's Bible
- **Evidence**: Published in Geneva, 1560; numerous editions through 1644

### Olivétan's French Bible (1535)
- **ID**: `object-olivetan-bible`
- **Title**: La Bible
- **Creator**: person-pierre-robert-olivetan
- **Date**: 1535
- **Type**: translation
- **Language**: French
- **Significance**: First French Bible translated from original Hebrew and Greek. Commissioned by Waldensians. Calvin wrote Latin and French prefaces. Basis for French Protestant Bibles.
- **Translation Philosophy**: From Hebrew and Greek originals; clear French prose
- **Connections**: Calvin (cousin) wrote preface; Olivétan studied Hebrew with Bucer and Capito in Strasbourg
- **Evidence**: Published in Neuchâtel, 1535; revised by Calvin 1540

### Zurich Bible (1531)
- **ID**: `object-zurich-bible`
- **Title**: Die gantze Bibel
- **Creator**: Leo Jud, Zwingli, and Zurich clergy
- **Date**: 1531
- **Type**: translation
- **Language**: German (Swiss German)
- **Significance**: Complete Swiss German Bible. Produced by Zwingli's team before his death. Different translation philosophy from Luther.
- **Translation Philosophy**: More literal than Luther; Swiss German dialect
- **Connections**: Zwingli supervised until his death (1531); Leo Jud primary translator
- **Evidence**: Published in Zurich by Froschauer, 1531

## Confessions and Catechisms

### Augsburg Confession (1530)
- **ID**: `object-augsburg-confession`
- **Title**: Confessio Augustana
- **Creator**: person-philip-melanchthon
- **Date**: June 25, 1530
- **Type**: confession
- **Language**: Latin and German
- **Significance**: Primary confession of Lutheran churches. Presented to Charles V at Diet of Augsburg. Moderate tone seeking reconciliation while defining Protestant doctrine.
- **Structure**: 28 articles: 21 on doctrine, 7 on abuses requiring reform
- **Key Doctrines**: Justification by faith; real presence in Eucharist; use of vernacular; clergy marriage
- **Connections**: Luther approved from Coburg Castle; Bucer's Tetrapolitan Confession also presented; Eck wrote Confutatio in response
- **Evidence**: Presented to Emperor Charles V at Augsburg, June 25, 1530; foundational Lutheran document

### Apology of the Augsburg Confession (1531)
- **ID**: `object-apology-augsburg`
- **Title**: Apologia Confessionis Augustanae
- **Creator**: person-philip-melanchthon
- **Date**: 1531
- **Type**: confession
- **Language**: Latin
- **Significance**: Melanchthon's detailed defense and expansion of the Augsburg Confession, responding to Catholic Confutatio. More detailed theological treatment.
- **Key Arguments**: Extended defense of justification by faith; detailed response to Eck's Confutatio
- **Connections**: Response to Catholic Confutatio; expanded Lutheran theological position
- **Evidence**: Published 1531; included in Book of Concord (1580)

### Small Catechism (1529)
- **ID**: `object-small-catechism`
- **Title**: Der kleine Katechismus
- **Creator**: person-martin-luther
- **Date**: 1529
- **Type**: catechism
- **Language**: German
- **Significance**: Luther's instruction manual for ordinary Christians. Covers Ten Commandments, Creed, Lord's Prayer, sacraments. Still used in Lutheran churches.
- **Structure**: Six chief parts: Commandments, Creed, Lord's Prayer, Baptism, Confession, Lord's Supper
- **Connections**: Produced after visitation revealed poor religious knowledge; used with Large Catechism
- **Evidence**: Published 1529; one of most printed books in German history

### Large Catechism (1529)
- **ID**: `object-large-catechism`
- **Title**: Der große Katechismus
- **Creator**: person-martin-luther
- **Date**: 1529
- **Type**: catechism
- **Language**: German
- **Significance**: Extended catechetical instruction for pastors and educated laity. More detailed than Small Catechism with expanded explanations.
- **Structure**: Same six parts as Small Catechism with extensive commentary
- **Connections**: Companion to Small Catechism; for clergy and teachers
- **Evidence**: Published 1529; included in Book of Concord (1580)

### Tetrapolitan Confession (1530)
- **ID**: `object-tetrapolitan-confession`
- **Title**: Confessio Tetrapolitana
- **Creator**: person-martin-bucer, person-wolfgang-capito
- **Date**: July 1530
- **Type**: confession
- **Language**: Latin
- **Significance**: Confession of four South German cities (Strasbourg, Constance, Memmingen, Lindau) at Augsburg. Attempted to mediate between Lutheran and Zwinglian positions on Eucharist.
- **Key Feature**: Mediating position on real presence; submitted alongside Augsburg Confession
- **Connections**: Bucer and Capito drafted; attempt at Protestant unity; cities later accepted Augsburg Confession
- **Evidence**: Presented at Diet of Augsburg, July 1530

### First Helvetic Confession (1536)
- **ID**: `object-first-helvetic-confession`
- **Title**: Confessio Helvetica Prior / Zweites Helvetisches Bekenntnis
- **Creator**: Heinrich Bullinger, Leo Jud, and others
- **Date**: 1536
- **Type**: confession
- **Language**: Latin and German
- **Significance**: First common confession of Swiss Reformed churches. Attempted to find common ground with Lutherans on Eucharist. Basis for later Reformed confessions.
- **Key Feature**: Moderate Reformed position; basis for Wittenberg Concord negotiations
- **Connections**: Bullinger primary author; attempt at Protestant unity
- **Evidence**: Adopted by Swiss Reformed churches, 1536

### Second Helvetic Confession (1566)
- **ID**: `object-second-helvetic-confession`
- **Title**: Confessio Helvetica Posterior
- **Creator**: person-heinrich-bullinger
- **Date**: 1566
- **Type**: confession
- **Language**: Latin
- **Significance**: Bullinger's personal confession, adopted by Swiss and many other Reformed churches. Most widely adopted Reformed confession of 16th century.
- **Key Feature**: Comprehensive Reformed theology; widely adopted in Scotland, Hungary, Poland
- **Connections**: Written by Bullinger; adopted after Zurich plague (1566)
- **Evidence**: Published 1566; adopted by most Reformed churches outside Germany

### Genevan Catechism (1542)
- **ID**: `object-genevan-catechism`
- **Title**: Le Catéchisme de l'Église de Genève
- **Creator**: person-john-calvin
- **Date**: 1542
- **Type**: catechism
- **Language**: French (also Latin)
- **Significance**: Calvin's catechism for Genevan church. Question-and-answer format covering Reformed doctrine. Used for instruction before communion.
- **Structure**: Covers faith, law, prayer, sacraments
- **Connections**: Used in Geneva; model for other Reformed catechisms
- **Evidence**: Published 1542; replaced earlier 1537 catechism

### Heidelberg Catechism (1563)
- **ID**: `object-heidelberg-catechism`
- **Title**: Heidelberger Katechismus
- **Creator**: Zacharias Ursinus and Caspar Olevianus
- **Date**: 1563
- **Type**: catechism
- **Language**: German
- **Significance**: Warmly personal Reformed catechism. 129 questions organized around guilt, grace, and gratitude. Widely used in Reformed churches.
- **Key Feature**: Pastoral tone; three-part structure (misery, deliverance, gratitude)
- **Connections**: Commissioned by Elector Frederick III; influenced by both Calvin and Melanchthon
- **Evidence**: Published 1563; included in Reformed confessional standards
- **Note**: Slightly beyond primary temporal scope but directly connected to Reformed tradition

### Schleitheim Confession (1527)
- **ID**: `object-schleitheim-confession`
- **Title**: Brüderlich Vereinigung / Schleitheim Articles
- **Creator**: person-michael-sattler
- **Date**: February 24, 1527
- **Type**: confession
- **Language**: German
- **Significance**: Foundational Anabaptist statement. Seven articles defining Swiss Brethren beliefs on baptism, ban, Lord's Supper, separation, pastors, sword, and oaths.
- **Key Doctrines**: Adult baptism only; church discipline (ban); nonresistance; separation from world
- **Connections**: Sattler primary author; defined Anabaptist identity against both Catholics and magisterial Reformers
- **Evidence**: Adopted at Schleitheim, February 1527; Sattler executed three months later

### Scots Confession (1560)
- **ID**: `object-scots-confession`
- **Title**: The Scots Confession
- **Creator**: person-john-knox and five others ("Six Johns")
- **Date**: August 1560
- **Type**: confession
- **Language**: Scots English
- **Significance**: Foundational confession of the Church of Scotland. Reformed theology with Calvinist influence. Established Presbyterian doctrine in Scotland.
- **Key Feature**: 25 articles; strong Calvinist influence; anti-Catholic polemic
- **Connections**: Knox primary author; adopted by Scottish Parliament
- **Evidence**: Adopted by Scottish Parliament, August 1560; replaced by Westminster Confession (1647)

### Thirty-Nine Articles (1563)
- **ID**: `object-thirty-nine-articles`
- **Title**: The Thirty-Nine Articles of Religion
- **Creator**: Matthew Parker and others (based on Cranmer's Forty-Two Articles)
- **Date**: 1563 (finalized 1571)
- **Type**: confession
- **Language**: English and Latin
- **Significance**: Doctrinal statement of Church of England. Moderate Protestant theology avoiding extremes. Deliberately ambiguous on some points to accommodate diversity.
- **Key Feature**: Reformed soteriology; episcopal governance; ambiguous on predestination and Eucharist
- **Connections**: Based on Cranmer's Forty-Two Articles (1553); Parker revised after Mary I
- **Evidence**: Adopted by Convocation 1563; confirmed by Parliament 1571

## Liturgical Works

### Book of Common Prayer (1549)
- **ID**: `object-bcp-1549`
- **Title**: The Booke of the Common Prayer and Administration of the Sacramentes
- **Creator**: person-thomas-cranmer
- **Date**: 1549
- **Type**: liturgy
- **Language**: English
- **Significance**: First vernacular prayer book for Church of England. Unified liturgy for entire nation. Conservative Protestant theology, retaining traditional structure.
- **Key Features**: English language; simplified ceremonies; eucharistic prayer allowing various interpretations
- **Connections**: Cranmer primary author; Bucer critiqued it (Censura); led to 1552 revision
- **Evidence**: Authorized by Act of Uniformity, 1549

### Book of Common Prayer (1552)
- **ID**: `object-bcp-1552`
- **Title**: The Booke of Common Prayer
- **Creator**: person-thomas-cranmer
- **Date**: 1552
- **Type**: liturgy
- **Language**: English
- **Significance**: Revised prayer book with more clearly Protestant theology. Removed references to real presence. Black Rubric added. In use only briefly before Mary I.
- **Key Changes**: More Protestant Eucharist; removal of vestments; "Black Rubric" on kneeling
- **Connections**: Incorporated Bucer's Censura; Ridley influenced changes; brief use before Mary I
- **Evidence**: Authorized by second Act of Uniformity, 1552; suspended by Mary I (1553)

### Strasbourg Liturgy
- **ID**: `object-strasbourg-liturgy`
- **Title**: Strasbourg Liturgical Forms
- **Creator**: person-martin-bucer
- **Date**: 1524-1539
- **Type**: liturgy
- **Language**: German
- **Significance**: Reformed liturgy that influenced both Calvin's Genevan liturgy and Cranmer's Prayer Book. Model for Protestant worship.
- **Key Features**: Vernacular worship; congregational participation; simplified ceremonies
- **Connections**: Influenced Calvin (who served in Strasbourg 1538-1541); influenced Cranmer's Prayer Book
- **Evidence**: Various editions from 1524; Calvin adapted for Geneva

### Genevan Psalter (1562)
- **ID**: `object-genevan-psalter`
- **Title**: Les Pseaumes mis en rime française
- **Creator**: person-clement-marot, Theodore Beza (completion), Louis Bourgeois (music)
- **Date**: 1562 (complete edition)
- **Type**: hymnal
- **Language**: French
- **Significance**: Complete metrical Psalter for Reformed worship. Marot translated 49 Psalms; Beza completed the rest. Music by Louis Bourgeois. Defined Reformed musical worship.
- **Musical Contribution**: Distinctive psalm tunes used across Europe; "Old Hundredth" from this collection
- **Connections**: Calvin sponsored; Marot began (49 Psalms); Beza completed; Bourgeois composed tunes
- **Evidence**: Complete 1562 edition; earlier partial editions from 1539

### German Hymns (Luther)
- **ID**: `object-luther-hymns`
- **Title**: Various hymns including "Ein feste Burg ist unser Gott"
- **Creator**: person-martin-luther
- **Date**: 1523-1545
- **Type**: hymnal
- **Language**: German
- **Significance**: Luther wrote approximately 37 hymns that established German Protestant hymnody. "A Mighty Fortress" became the anthem of the Reformation.
- **Key Hymns**: "Ein feste Burg" (A Mighty Fortress); "Vom Himmel hoch" (From Heaven Above); "Christ lag in Todes Banden"
- **Connections**: Collected in various hymnals; established congregational singing as Protestant distinctive
- **Evidence**: Various hymnal collections from 1524 onward

## Polemical and Historical Works

### Actes and Monuments (Book of Martyrs) (1563)
- **ID**: `object-book-of-martyrs`
- **Title**: Actes and Monuments of these Latter and Perillous Days
- **Creator**: person-john-foxe
- **Date**: 1563 (expanded editions 1570, 1576, 1583)
- **Type**: history
- **Language**: English
- **Significance**: Comprehensive martyrology of Protestant martyrs. Shaped English Protestant identity and anti-Catholic sentiment for centuries. Ordered placed in churches alongside Bible.
- **Key Content**: Accounts of Marian martyrs (Cranmer, Latimer, Ridley); history of persecution; woodcut illustrations
- **Connections**: Documents Cranmer, Latimer, Ridley, and other English martyrs; anti-papal historiography
- **Evidence**: First edition 1563; greatly expanded 1570 edition; reprinted for centuries

### The Praise of Folly (1511)
- **ID**: `object-praise-of-folly`
- **Title**: Moriae Encomium
- **Creator**: person-erasmus
- **Date**: 1511
- **Type**: satire
- **Language**: Latin
- **Significance**: Erasmus's satirical critique of church corruption and scholastic theology. Written for Thomas More. Laid groundwork for Reformation critique while remaining formally Catholic.
- **Key Themes**: Satirizes monks, theologians, popes; humanist critique of medieval Christianity
- **Connections**: Written for Thomas More (punning on "moria"=folly and "More"); influenced reform sentiment
- **Evidence**: Published 1511; numerous editions; widely influential

### Enchiridion (1503)
- **ID**: `object-enchiridion`
- **Title**: Enchiridion militis Christiani (Handbook of a Christian Soldier)
- **Creator**: person-erasmus
- **Date**: 1503
- **Type**: devotional/treatise
- **Language**: Latin
- **Significance**: Erasmus's program for lay Christian life emphasizing inner piety over external ceremonies. Blueprint for Christian humanism. Influenced reform sentiment.
- **Key Arguments**: Inner religion over external observance; Scripture and Church Fathers as guides; Christ as model
- **Connections**: Influenced evangelical humanism; widely read across Europe
- **Evidence**: Published 1503; became bestseller after 1515 reprinting

### Supplication to Henry VIII (1531)
- **ID**: `object-barnes-supplication`
- **Title**: A Supplication unto the Most Gracious Prince King Henry VIII
- **Creator**: person-robert-barnes
- **Date**: 1531
- **Type**: treatise
- **Language**: English
- **Significance**: Barnes's appeal to Henry VIII for reform. Lutheran influence on English reform. Barnes later executed.
- **Key Arguments**: Appeal for evangelical reform; Lutheran theology
- **Connections**: Barnes connected Lutheran and English reform; patron Cromwell

- **ID**: `object-erasmus-correspondence`
